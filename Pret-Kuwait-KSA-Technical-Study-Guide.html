01 Franchise Royalty Accounting

The concept: a franchisee pays the franchisor (Pret UK/Asia entity) an ongoing royalty, almost always calculated as a percentage of net sales (sales net of VAT/discounts, not gross till receipts). This is the franchisor's recurring return on the brand licence, separate from the one-off initial franchise fee.

How it's accounted for

Royalty is expensed in the period the related sales occur — it's a direct, variable operating cost, not capitalised. It typically sits in the P&L between gross profit and store EBITDA, often shown as its own line so management can see "store contribution before brand cost" separately.

Worked example — monthly royalty accrual, single shop
ItemAmount (KWD)
Net sales for the month (Shop: Al Hamra Tower)42,000
Royalty rate per franchise agreement6%
Royalty payable2,520

Illustrative journal entry at month-end:

Dr Royalty Expense (P&L) 2,520 Cr Royalty Payable — Pret Franchisor (BS) 2,520 Being royalty accrued at 6% of net sales for [month] On payment (net of any Kuwait 5% retention — see Section 4): Dr Royalty Payable — Pret Franchisor 2,520 Cr Tax Retention Payable — KTA (5%) 126 Cr Bank 2,394

What to be ready to discuss

  • Whether royalty is calculated on a tiered basis (some agreements step down the rate above certain sales thresholds) — worth asking if you don't know the actual structure
  • Timing: accrued monthly against actual sales, vs. paid on a different cycle (e.g., paid quarterly in arrears) — creates a payable balance to track
  • Currency: if invoiced in GBP, you're translating KWD sales into a GBP royalty obligation — an FX translation step before the retention/WHT calculation even begins

02 Initial Franchise Fee — Capitalisation & Amortisation

The concept: when a new shop opens, the franchisee typically pays a one-off, non-recurring initial franchise fee to the franchisor for the right to operate under the brand at that location. Unlike royalty, this is capitalised as an intangible asset and amortised over the life of the franchise agreement for that unit (commonly 5–10 years, matching the contract term).

Worked example — new shop opening
ItemAmount (KWD)
Initial franchise fee (per new shop)30,000
Franchise agreement term for this unit10 years
Annual amortisation (straight-line)3,000
Monthly amortisation charge250
On payment / shop opening: Dr Intangible Asset — Franchise Rights 30,000 Cr Bank 30,000 Monthly amortisation: Dr Amortisation Expense (P&L) 250 Cr Accumulated Amortisation — Franchise Rights 250

What to be ready to discuss

  • Impairment testing — if a shop underperforms badly or closes early, the remaining unamortised franchise fee balance needs an impairment review, similar logic to the goodwill impairment Pret UK itself took in FY2024
  • Whether the fee is per-shop or a lump-sum development fee covering a multi-unit development schedule (common in master franchise agreements) — the latter would be capitalised and allocated across the planned shop openings

03 Marketing / Brand Fund Accounting

The concept: many franchise agreements require a separate contribution — often 1–3% of net sales — into a marketing/brand fund, either pooled regionally by the franchisor or ring-fenced for local market spend. The accounting question that always comes up: gross or net presentation?

Gross vs. Net Presentation
ApproachWhen it appliesP&L impact
Gross (agent model)Franchisee collects/forwards the fund and has no discretion over how it's spent — acting as a collection agent for the franchisor's regional marketing potContribution shown as a pass-through; doesn't inflate the franchisee's own marketing expense line
Net (principal model)Franchisee retains the fund and directs local marketing spend itself, subject to brand guidelinesContribution is recognised as the franchisee's own marketing expense as spent, not as an immediate pass-through liability

  • Whether unspent marketing fund balances roll forward or are forfeited at year-end — affects whether you carry a liability or recognise income on lapse
  • Whether any portion is pooled regionally (across Kuwait + KSA + historically Qatar) vs. spent purely locally — relevant given the two-market scope of this role

04 Kuwait 5% Tax Retention — Full Mechanics

The legal basis: Law No. 2 of 2008 requires any party in Kuwait contracting with a foreign (non-Kuwaiti) entity to retain 5% of each payment due under that contract, until the foreign party obtains a No Liability Certificate (NOL) from the Kuwait Tax Authority (KTA) confirming its Kuwait tax position is clear.

Why it applies here

Pret's franchisor entity (UK or Asia-based) is a foreign party receiving ongoing payments — royalty, marketing fund, and the initial franchise fee — from a Kuwaiti contracting entity (One PM / the Kuwait operating company). That's squarely within scope.

The mechanics, step by step

  1. KTA registration — the Kuwaiti contracting party (One PM's Kuwait entity) must register the contract with KTA, generally within a defined window after signing
  2. Retention at source — on every payment to the franchisor, the Kuwaiti entity retains 5% rather than remitting it
  3. Foreign party's compliance — the franchisor (or its local tax agent) files with KTA to demonstrate it has no outstanding Kuwait tax liability — this can take time, sometimes spanning multiple payment cycles
  4. NOL issued — once KTA is satisfied, it issues the No Liability Certificate
  5. Release of retention — accumulated retained amounts are released to the franchisor (or, if KTA finds a liability, applied against it first)
Worked example — annual royalty payments with retention

If the NOL is obtained in, say, March of the following year, the full KWD 6,125 retained balance is released to the franchisor at that point (assuming no liability was found).

Each quarter, on payment: Dr Royalty Payable — Franchisor 28,000 Cr Tax Retention Payable — KTA 1,400 Cr Bank 26,600 On NOL issuance and release: Dr Tax Retention Payable — KTA 6,125 Cr Bank 6,125
Talking point — this is where you stand out Be ready to say plainly: the retention is a cash flow timing issue for the franchisor, not a cost to the Kuwaiti entity — but it creates an ongoing liability balance you must track, reconcile, and manage proactively so it doesn't sit stale on the balance sheet for years. A well-run finance function chases the NOL actively rather than letting retained balances accumulate indefinitely, since franchisors increasingly expect their Kuwaiti partners to manage this efficiently as part of the relationship.

05 KSA Withholding Tax (ZATCA) — Full Mechanics

The legal basis: Saudi Arabia's income tax law requires withholding tax (WHT) on payments made by a Saudi-resident entity to a non-resident party for Saudi-source income — royalties, licence fees, technical/management services, and similar. Administered by ZATCA (Zakat, Tax and Customs Authority).

How it differs structurally from Kuwait's retention

Kuwait Retention vs. KSA WHT
FeatureKuwait (Law 2/2008)KSA WHT
NatureTemporary retention, released on clearanceFinal tax, deducted and remitted — not "held," genuinely withheld
RateFlat 5%Varies by payment category — royalties commonly 15%; technical/consulting services 5%; management fees can differ — confirm actual contract classification
Clearance mechanismNOL certificate required to releaseNo release mechanism — it's remitted to ZATCA monthly, full stop
Treaty reliefGenerally not available in practiceAvailable if franchisor obtains a Certificate of Tax Residence and the UK-Saudi treaty reduces the applicable rate
Worked example — monthly royalty payment from KSA entity
ItemAmount (SAR)
Net sales, KSA shops for the month155,000
Royalty rate6%
Royalty due9,300
WHT rate applied (royalty classification, treaty not yet documented)15%
WHT withheld & remitted to ZATCA1,395
Net paid to franchisor7,905
Dr Royalty Expense (P&L) 9,300 Cr Royalty Payable — Franchisor 9,300 On payment: Dr Royalty Payable — Franchisor 9,300 Cr WHT Payable — ZATCA 1,395 Cr Bank 7,905 On monthly WHT remittance to ZATCA: Dr WHT Payable — ZATCA 1,395 Cr Bank 1,395
Talking point Contrast this directly with Kuwait when asked: in KSA there's no pending liability account waiting on a certificate — WHT is remitted and the matter is closed each month, unless treaty relief is later claimed (which would typically apply prospectively, not retroactively, once the franchisor provides a valid Certificate of Tax Residence). That's a materially simpler cash mechanic than Kuwait's retention, even though the headline rate is higher.

06 KSA Zakat — Full Mechanics

The concept: Saudi/GCC-owned entities (or the Saudi/GCC-owned portion of a mixed-ownership entity) operating in KSA are subject to Zakat instead of corporate income tax on that ownership share, calculated on a Zakat base broadly built from net worth/equity-style adjustments — not on net profit the way income tax is.

Why this matters for a One PM KSA entity

If the KSA operating entity is wholly or partly owned by GCC nationals/entities (plausible, given One PM's Kuwaiti ownership under Al-Sayer Franchising), the GCC-owned equity portion is subject to Zakat, while any non-GCC foreign ownership portion (if any) would be subject to corporate income tax instead. A mixed-ownership structure means both regimes can apply simultaneously, split by ownership percentage.

Worked example — simplified Zakat base calculation
ItemAmount (SAR)
Shareholders' equity (opening, per audited financials)2,400,000
Add: long-term liabilities financing fixed assets350,000
Less: net book value of fixed assets & investments(1,100,000)
Less: deductions (e.g., certain provisions, per ZATCA rules)(80,000)
Zakat base1,570,000
Zakat rate2.5%
Zakat payable (on the GCC-owned % of equity)39,250

If the entity is, say, 100% Kuwaiti (GCC)-owned, the full SAR 39,250 applies as Zakat with no separate corporate income tax layer. If there were a 20% non-GCC foreign shareholder, that 20% slice of profit would instead be taxed at the standard KSA corporate income tax rate (20%), while the 80% GCC-owned slice carries Zakat.

Talking point You don't need the exact ownership split memorised — what matters is showing you know Zakat is base-driven (equity-style), not profit-driven, which surprises people coming from a pure income-tax background, and that it runs in parallel with — not instead of — the WHT mechanics on franchisor payments covered in Section 5. The two are unrelated: Zakat taxes the KSA entity's own ownership structure; WHT taxes payments flowing out to the foreign franchisor.

07 Multi-Entity Consolidation — Kuwait + KSA

The concept: with operating entities in two countries under one master franchise, group reporting requires combining both into a single consolidated view — translating KSA's SAR results into KWD, eliminating any intercompany balances, and presenting a combined P&L and balance sheet for One PM Holding (or whatever the top entity is).

Core mechanics

  • Currency translation — KSA's SAR P&L translated at average rate for the period; balance sheet at closing rate; resulting translation difference parked in a Foreign Currency Translation Reserve within equity, not run through the P&L
  • Intercompany eliminations — if Kuwait charges KSA (or vice versa) for shared services, management fees, or central procurement, those balances and the related revenue/expense must be eliminated on consolidation so the group doesn't double-count
  • Uniform accounting policies — both entities should apply the same revenue recognition, inventory valuation, and depreciation policies before consolidation; reconcile any local statutory adjustments back to group policy
Worked example — simplified consolidation

Net sales280,000155,00012,710292,710
Royalty expense16,8009,30076317,563
Less: intercompany management fee (Kuwait charges KSA)(4,500)4,500*3690 (eliminated)

*Shown as the SAR-equivalent of the KWD 4,500 management fee Kuwait charges KSA — eliminated on consolidation so it doesn't appear as both Kuwait income and KSA expense in the group numbers. Illustrative FX rate used: 1 SAR ≈ 0.082 KWD.

What to be ready to discuss

  • Whether Kuwait and KSA run on a single ERP instance or separate systems requiring a manual consolidation workbook — directly affects how much of this is automated vs. manual reconciliation work
  • Segment reporting — management likely wants Kuwait and KSA shown separately (not just blended) given they're at different maturity stages
  • How royalty, marketing fund, and tax treatment differences between the two markets are reconciled into one set of group management accounts without losing market-level visibility

The Currency Picture

What to be ready to discuss

  • A natural hedge argument is weak here — Kuwait/KSA revenue is in KWD/SAR, royalty obligations are in GBP, so there's no offsetting GBP-denominated inflow to net against
  • For a business this size, forward contracts are likely only justified for known, material payment dates (e.g., quarterly royalty settlements) rather than continuous hedging — proportionality matters
  • Budget-rate variance reporting (actual vs. budget FX rate impact, isolated from operational variance) is the practical minimum control even without active hedging

10 COGS & Food Cost Management

The concept: in F&B retail, COGS (food, coffee, packaging) is tracked obsessively as a percentage of net sales — "food cost %" — because it's the largest controllable variable cost after labour, and small leakages (waste, portioning, theft) compound quickly across many shops.

Worked example — food cost variance
ItemAmount (KWD)
Net sales42,000
Target food cost % (per recipe-costed menu)26.0%
Target COGS10,920
Actual COGS incurred11,760
Unfavourable variance840 (2.0 pts)

Where to look: imported coffee/ingredient price inflation, portion control at shop level, waste from unsold perishables (Pret's "made today, sold today" model means unsold stock is donated, not sold down — a deliberate brand commitment that has a real cost trade-off worth understanding), or a shift in sales mix toward higher-cost items.

What to be ready to discuss

  • Landed cost build-up for imported coffee/ingredients — FOB price + freight + Kuwait/KSA customs duty + clearance costs, all flowing into standard cost
  • The donation-not-discount model (refood partnership in Kuwait) means waste is a fixed cost of the brand promise, not a variable to be optimised away — a nuance worth knowing if asked about margin levers

12 Percentage Rent Lease Accounting

The concept: many GCC mall leases for F&B tenants combine a base (minimum guaranteed) rent with an additional percentage-of-sales rent once the shop's sales exceed a breakpoint — the landlord shares directly in upside.

Worked example — percentage rent calculation
ItemAmount (KWD)
Minimum guaranteed rent (monthly)4,000
Natural breakpoint (rent ÷ percentage rate)—
Percentage rent rate above breakpoint8%
Breakpoint sales level (4,000 ÷ 8%)50,000
Actual net sales this month42,000
Rent payable (sales below breakpoint → minimum applies)4,000

If sales had instead been KWD 58,000: percentage rent = 8% × 58,000 = KWD 4,640, which exceeds the KWD 4,000 minimum, so KWD 4,640 would be payable instead. The higher of the two always applies.

What to be ready to discuss

  • Under IFRS 16, the minimum guaranteed rent is the fixed lease payment used to calculate the right-of-use asset and lease liability; percentage rent above the minimum is typically treated as variable lease payment, expensed as incurred rather than capitalised into the lease liability
  • Why this matters for store-opening decisions — a high breakpoint protects margin upside in strong locations, but a poorly negotiated breakpoint can mean the landlord captures most of the growth from a shop that outperforms expectations

15 Working Capital in F&B Retail

F&B retail is structurally working-capital-light compared to most businesses — cash/card sales mean minimal receivables, but perishable inventory (especially Pret's "made fresh daily, nothing kept overnight" model) means very fast inventory turns and tight supplier terms management.

Worked example — inventory turn
ItemAmount (KWD)
Annual COGS, all Kuwait shops (illustrative)988,000
Average inventory on hand19,000
Inventory turns per year~52x (roughly weekly)

A ~52x annual turn (roughly once a week) reflects perishables — coffee/packaging can carry slightly longer stock cover than fresh sandwich/bakery components, which may turn daily. Worth noting this isn't a single blended number in practice; perishables and shelf-stable inputs should be tracked separately.

ComponentTypical GCC F&B retail behaviour
ReceivablesMinimal — cash/card sales settle quickly; aggregator platforms (Talabat/Deliveroo) typically settle on a short cycle (e.g., weekly/bi-weekly), creating a small but trackable receivable
InventoryVery fast turn on perishables; longer cover on imported/shelf-stable items (coffee beans, packaging) to manage freight lead times
PayablesNegotiated supplier terms (e.g., 30–60 days) on imported goods provide a natural working capital benefit, partially offsetting the franchisor payment cycle

16 Glossary / Quick Reference

NOL (No Liability Certificate)
KTA-issued certificate confirming a foreign party has no outstanding Kuwait tax liability; required to release amounts retained under Law 2/2008.
ZATCA
Zakat, Tax and Customs Authority — Saudi Arabia's combined tax/customs/Zakat regulator.
Zakat base
Equity-style calculation (broadly: equity + long-term liabilities financing fixed assets − net fixed assets − certain deductions) used to compute Zakat, distinct from a profit-based tax base.
Master franchisee
The entity holding franchise rights for a defined territory, with the ability to operate directly and/or grant sub-franchise rights to others within that territory (as One PM did with Al Mana for Qatar).
Percentage rent breakpoint
The sales level at which percentage rent (% of sales) equals the minimum guaranteed rent; above this point, percentage rent exceeds the minimum and becomes payable instead.
IFRS 16
Lease accounting standard requiring most leases to be capitalised as a right-of-use asset and lease liability on the balance sheet, rather than expensed as operating rent.
Translation reserve
Equity account capturing FX differences from translating a foreign subsidiary's (e.g., KSA's) financial statements into the group's presentation currency (KWD) — does not flow through the P&L.
Natural hedge
When a business's foreign-currency costs are offset by foreign-currency revenue in the same currency, reducing the need for active hedging — largely absent here, since KWD/SAR revenue doesn't naturally offset a GBP royalty obligation.
