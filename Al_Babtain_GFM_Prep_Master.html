Q1.3GCFOWhat do you actually know about how FMCG finance differs from diversified holding company finance — rebates, trade spend, slotting fees, sell-in vs sell-out?

Why he's asking: Testing real domain knowledge versus willingness to learn — don't confuse the two in your answer.

Approach A — Show the IFRS foundation, name the gap honestly

Define it correctly in your own words: sell-in is revenue when goods leave the distributor to the retailer; sell-out is when the retailer sells to the end consumer, and FMCG distributors are managed on both simultaneously. Trade spend and rebates are variable consideration under IFRS 15 — accrued against revenue at point of sale based on expected redemption, not expensed when cash moves. Slotting fees are typically treated as a reduction of revenue, not a marketing expense. Then be direct: "The accounting principle is one I know cold. The commercial vocabulary and category-specific judgment calls around it are new — and that's a fast study, not a steep one."

Approach B — Bridge from adjacent experience

Connect to real experience: managing complex revenue recognition and accrual judgment across multiple business lines in a diversified holding structure already builds the muscle — FMCG just adds specific mechanics on top of a discipline you already practise daily.

Anchor line"It's the same accounting principle — variable consideration under IFRS 15. What's new is the vocabulary around it, not the concept."

Q2.1GCFOMassad and Raad import from 25+ and 9 countries. How would you handle FX exposure on a multi-currency import book in KWD?

Why he's asking: Treasury thinking specific to an import-driven distribution model — generic textbook hedging answers won't land.

Approach A — Identify the real exposure first

The actual risk isn't broad "FX volatility" — most of Massad's dairy/beverage portfolio (Elle & Vire, Corman, Kerrygold) is EUR-sourced, so the real exposure is EUR/KWD, not USD, since KWD's basket peg already dampens USD swings. Frame the bigger practical risk as a lag risk, not a currency risk: supplier price increases passed through late erode margin more than spot FX moves. Propose a quarterly price-review mechanism with contractual margin-floor protection as the primary tool, with forward cover as a secondary layer.

Anchor line"Cover the committed exposure, not the speculative one — and put the discipline in the contract, not just the treasury desk."
Q2.2GCFOKuwait's 5% withholding under Law No. 2/2008 — how does that interact with an import-heavy FMCG model versus a service contract?

Why he's asking: Direct test of applied, not theoretical, GCC tax knowledge — and you're already well-versed here.

Approach A — Draw the line precisely

5% retention applies to payments to foreign entities for services or contracts performed in Kuwait — not to goods import transactions, which sit under customs/duty rules instead. For Massad and Raad, the bulk of spend is goods purchase (no WHT trigger), but service contracts to non-Kuwaiti vendors — consulting, marketing agencies, IT/Oracle support — do trigger the 5% retention, requiring KTA registration and a valid NOL/TCC from the vendor before final payment release.

Approach B — Flag the highest-risk payment stream

Q2.3GCFOTazweed runs a franchise model with Lavazza. What's different about franchise accounting that you haven't dealt with before?

Why he's asking: Genuinely new technical territory for you — credibility here comes from honesty plus framework thinking, not false familiarity.

Approach A — Show the technical framework

Initial franchise fees are typically capitalised as an intangible right and amortised over the franchise term; ongoing royalty/management fees are expensed as incurred, usually a percentage of store revenue. The real shift is needing store-level four-wall P&Ls — COGS, labour, occupancy, royalty — separate from group consolidation, to judge unit economics before any expansion decision.

Approach B — Honest gap, bridged by transferable skill

"Multi-unit franchise accounting specifically is new to me — but building cost-centre P&Ls and rolling them into group reporting is exactly what I do today across seven companies. I'd want store-level four-wall visibility within the first month before touching any expansion call on the KSA rollout."

Anchor line"Tell me the four-wall economics of one Lavazza store, and I'll tell you whether the next ten make sense."

Why he's asking: Another genuine gap — same playbook as Q2.3: name it, then show the control-design instinct underneath.

Shorter shelf-life inventory needs FEFO (first-expired-first-out) discipline, higher-frequency cycle counts, and a formal wastage/shrinkage provisioning policy tied to historical loss rates by SKU category — dairy, beverages, ambient goods each behave differently — with wastage reported as its own P&L line, not buried inside COGS where it goes unmanaged.

Anchor line"I won't pretend I've run cold-chain inventory before — give me twelve months of wastage data and I'll have a defensible provisioning policy in two weeks."
Q2.5GCFOGive me one real example where you caught a control gap before it became a P&L problem.

Q2.6GCFOTrade spend and promotional accruals — accrual or cash basis, and how do you stop sales teams gaming the numbers?

Why he's asking: Classic FMCG control question — your accrual instincts are strong here even without sector-specific exposure.

Approach A — Accrual, with a control mechanism

Trade spend should always be accrued at the point of sale as variable consideration under IFRS 15, based on expected redemption/claim rates — cash-basis recognition is exactly how FMCG margins get overstated and then blown up by a surprise true-up six to twelve months later. To prevent gaming: cap accrual rates by category off historical claim data, require finance sign-off above a defined threshold, and run a quarterly reconciliation of accrued versus actual claims by customer and SKU with variance review.

Approach B — Tie to your existing control philosophy

Frame it as separation of duties you already practise: commercial proposes the spend, finance approves and tracks it against budget — the same discipline that underpins your P&L oversight today, just pointed at a new line item.

Anchor line"Unaccrued trade spend is a liability you haven't recognised yet — I'd rather over-accrue conservatively than get surprised by a true-up."

FX Exposure — Types & Management

IAS 21 · IFRS 9

Before hedging anything, you need to know which of three distinct exposures you're actually managing — they have different drivers, different time horizons, and different accounting treatment.

The three exposure types

Transaction exposure — risk on a specific, already-contracted transaction between the date you commit (PO/invoice) and the date you settle. This is cash-flow real: it changes what you actually pay or receive.

Translation exposure — arises purely on consolidation, when a foreign subsidiary's functional-currency statements are converted into the group's presentation currency (KWD). No cash moves; it affects reported equity through Other Comprehensive Income (OCI), not the P&L.

Economic exposure — the long-run effect of sustained FX movement on the present value of future cash flows and competitive position. A structurally stronger EUR over several years makes EUR-sourced goods permanently more expensive relative to a competitor sourcing elsewhere — margin erosion you can't hedge away with a forward contract.

For this group: Massad's exposure is dominated by EUR (Elle & Vire, Corman are French; Kerrygold is Irish) — not USD, which is the common assumption people make about Kuwait.

Why KWD/EUR is the real risk, not KWD/USD

Kuwaiti dinar has been pegged since 2007 to an undisclosed weighted basket of currencies, not a single currency — meaning KWD/USD tends to stay relatively narrow-banded, but KWD/EUR moves as a function of both the basket's weighting and the underlying EUR/USD cross-rate. The practical consequence: a group whose import book looks "safe" because it's denominated in a stable-seeming currency can still carry meaningful exposure if the underlying sourcing currency is EUR, not USD.

Q1Walk me through the three types of FX exposure and which one matters most for Massad and Raad specifically.

Transaction exposure is the day-to-day cash-flow risk between contracting and settling a specific purchase — for Massad, every EUR-denominated dairy PO carries this between order date and the 30-60 day payment term. Translation exposure only arises on consolidation, when a foreign operation's own-currency statements get converted into the group's KWD presentation currency — that's where Tazweed KSA's SAR-functional results come in, not Massad or Raad, since both are domestic KWD operations with no foreign subsidiary to translate. Economic exposure is the slow-burn one: if EUR strengthens structurally over several years against KWD, Massad's landed cost base shifts permanently versus any competitor sourcing dairy from a non-EUR zone — that's a pricing and sourcing-strategy conversation, not a treasury one.

For this group specifically, transaction exposure on Massad's EUR import book is the most immediate, measurable risk — and the one I'd build a hedging policy around first.

Q2Why would Massad's EUR exposure actually be riskier than a USD exposure, given Kuwait's dinar is often assumed to be USD-pegged?

That assumption is outdated — Kuwait moved off a pure USD peg in 2007 onto an undisclosed currency basket, so KWD/USD has stayed relatively narrow but isn't fixed, and KWD/EUR inherits volatility from two sources at once: the basket's own rebalancing and the underlying EUR/USD cross-rate. A EUR import book is therefore exposed to a compound movement, not a single clean rate. Practically, this means I wouldn't size a hedging programme off general "Kuwait is stable" assumptions — I'd pull twelve months of actual KWD/EUR closing rates, not KWD/USD, to size real volatility before deciding on hedge ratios.

Q3If I gave you a EUR 2 million annual import book spread evenly across the year, how would you design a hedging policy — what would you NOT hedge?

I'd hedge committed, already-contracted purchase orders with short-tenor forward contracts matched to actual payment dates — that's the genuine transaction exposure with a known amount and known date. I would not hedge forecast or anticipated future volume that isn't yet contracted, since that's a budget assumption, not an exposure, and over-hedging a forecast creates its own P&L volatility if the forecast doesn't materialise. I'd also build in a natural hedge first: negotiating margin-floor or price-review clauses into supplier agreements so a portion of FX movement passes through to selling price rather than sitting entirely on Massad's margin — cheaper than buying forward cover on the full book, and forward cover then only needs to address the residual, uncovered portion.

Q4What's the accounting difference between a formally designated cash flow hedge and just buying a forward contract without designation?

Without formal hedge designation under IFRS 9, the forward contract is measured at fair value through P&L every period — its mark-to-market gains and losses hit the income statement immediately, creating volatility that doesn't line up with when the underlying purchase actually happens. With a formally designated cash flow hedge — documented hedge relationship, hedge ratio, and effectiveness testing in place — the effective portion of the forward's fair value change is deferred in OCI (a cash flow hedge reserve) rather than the P&L, and only released when the hedged transaction occurs, typically as a basis adjustment to the cost of inventory. The ineffective portion, if any, still goes to P&L immediately. The practical upshot: formal designation requires more documentation up front but produces a P&L that actually reflects the economics of a hedged purchase programme instead of swinging on mark-to-market noise.

Multi-Currency Consolidation

IAS 21

This is the area to genuinely drill — it's where "I know the theory" and "I can actually do it" diverge fastest under questioning.

Functional currency — determine it first, always

IAS 21.9-11 sets primary indicators: the currency that mainly influences sales prices of goods and services (the currency competitive forces and regulation determine pricing in), and the currency that mainly influences labour, material, and other costs. Secondary indicators look at financing currency and the currency operating receipts are retained in.

For Tazweed KSA, this isn't ambiguous: local store pricing is in SAR, local rent and staff costs are in SAR — functional currency is SAR, full stop, even though the group reports in KWD. Functional currency is a fact pattern, not a policy election.

The current rate method — how translation actually works

Once functional currency (SAR) differs from presentation currency (KWD), IAS 21.39 requires:

Assets and liabilities — translated at the closing rate (rate at balance sheet date).
Income and expenses — translated at rates at the dates of the transactions, approximated in practice by an average rate for the period (IAS 21.40).
Equity (share capital, pre-acquisition reserves) — translated at the historical rate ruling when that equity arose.

The arithmetic mismatch between rolling equity forward using historical-plus-average rates versus translating the closing balance directly at the closing rate is the plug — the Currency Translation Adjustment (CTA), recognised in OCI as a separate translation reserve, not the P&L.

IAS 21.48 — on disposal of the foreign operation, the cumulative CTA is recycled out of OCI into P&L.

Q5If Tazweed KSA's functional currency is SAR, walk me through exactly how you'd translate its financial statements into the group's KWD reporting.

First confirm functional currency is genuinely SAR using IAS 21's primary indicators — pricing and cost currency — which it clearly is here. Then apply the current rate method: translate every balance sheet asset and liability at the closing rate on the reporting date; translate every P&L line at the average rate for the period, since transaction-date translation isn't practical at volume; translate share capital and any pre-acquisition reserves at the historical rate from when that equity originally arose. The arithmetic won't reconcile perfectly between a "rolled-forward" equity figure and a "closing-rate-translated" equity figure — that gap is the Currency Translation Adjustment, booked to a separate translation reserve within OCI, not the income statement. It sits there, cumulative, until the day we dispose of or liquidate the KSA entity.

Q6Why doesn't the CTA equal the FX gain or loss you'd compute on a simple cash account — what's actually driving the plug?

A simple cash account has one rate movement to track — opening rate versus closing rate on one balance. The CTA is structurally different because it's the byproduct of translating three different line items at three different rates in the same period: assets and liabilities at the closing rate, income and expenses at the average rate, and pre-existing equity at historical rates from whenever it arose. Those three rates almost never move in perfect proportion to each other within a period, so even a subsidiary with zero net cash movement will still generate a translation adjustment purely from the mismatch between average-rate-translated profit being added to historical-rate equity, and the whole thing then being checked against a closing-rate-translated balance sheet. It's a mechanical consequence of the standard, not a real economic gain or loss in the way a settled transaction is.

Q7If we sold Tazweed KSA next year, what happens to the translation reserve we've built up?

Under IAS 21.48, the cumulative translation reserve relating to that specific foreign operation is reclassified — "recycled" — out of OCI and into the P&L as part of the gain or loss on disposal, in the period of disposal. Until that point it sits quietly in equity, having no P&L impact at all; it only crystallises into the income statement the moment we lose control of the entity. That's worth knowing for deal modelling too — a multi-year accumulated translation loss on a foreign subsidiary becomes a real, recognised P&L hit at exit, not just a balance sheet footnote, so it should factor into any exit valuation work, not just statutory close.

Q8What's the difference between functional currency and presentation currency, and could they ever be the same for a foreign subsidiary?

Functional currency is a fact about the entity itself — determined by where its sales prices, costs, and financing are actually denominated, per IAS 21's indicators; it's not a choice. Presentation currency is the currency the group chooses to present its consolidated financial statements in — KWD here, because that's what's useful to the group's stakeholders, regardless of what currency any individual entity transacts in. They absolutely can be the same — Massad and Raad, both Kuwait-domestic businesses, have KWD as both their functional and the group's presentation currency, so no translation mechanics apply to them at all. It's only Tazweed KSA, where functional currency (SAR) diverges from presentation currency (KWD), that triggers the full current rate method and CTA mechanics described above.

Consolidation Mechanics

IFRS 10 · IFRS 3

Below the textbook definition of "control" is a mechanical worksheet process — and it's the worksheet, not the definition, that gets tested when someone asks you to actually do it.

Control, and the five-step consolidation worksheet

IFRS 10.7 defines control as exposure (or rights) to variable returns from an investee, combined with the power to affect those returns through existing rights over relevant activities. Once control is established, the mechanical process is always the same five steps:

1. Combine line by line — 100% of each subsidiary's assets, liabilities, income, and expenses, regardless of ownership percentage.
2. Eliminate the parent's "Investment in Subsidiary" against the subsidiary's equity at acquisition date — any excess paid is goodwill (IFRS 3); any shortfall is a bargain purchase gain to P&L.
3. Recognise Non-Controlling Interest (NCI) for the percentage not owned by the parent.
4. Eliminate all intercompany transactions — sales/purchases, receivables/payables, loans and related interest, dividends.
5. Eliminate unrealised profit sitting in intercompany inventory still on hand at period end.

Q9Walk me through, step by step, how you'd consolidate Massad and Raad into one set of group financials.

Both being wholly Kuwait-domestic, KWD-functional entities removes the translation layer entirely, so it's a clean five-step worksheet: combine every line of both entities' trial balances in full; eliminate the parent's investment account against each subsidiary's equity as it stood at acquisition, recognising goodwill or a bargain purchase gain for any difference; recognise NCI for any percentage not directly owned by the parent, if applicable; eliminate every intercompany receivable, payable, sale, purchase, and any management fee or interest charged between the two; and finally check whether either entity holds the other's goods in its closing inventory and strip out any unrealised intercompany margin still sitting there. The output is one set of statements that reflects the group as if it were a single economic entity transacting only with the outside world.

Q10What's the difference between proportionate NCI and full goodwill method, and which would you recommend for a closely-held family group like this?

The proportionate (partial goodwill) method values NCI at its percentage share of the subsidiary's identifiable net assets only, with goodwill recognised purely for the parent's share of consideration paid above its share of net assets. The full goodwill method instead measures NCI at fair value at acquisition — typically requiring a valuation exercise — which grosses up total goodwill to reflect 100% of the entity's implied value, not just the parent's purchased portion. For a closely-held family group with no near-term plan to sell down or list the NCI stake, I'd recommend the partial/proportionate method: it avoids an unnecessary fair-value exercise on a minority stake that isn't being actively traded, and it's the simpler, more commonly applied policy in practice for private groups. I'd still confirm whichever policy was used historically for Massad and Raad's original acquisitions and stay consistent with it rather than switching.

Q11If Raad sells inventory to Massad at a markup and it's still sitting in Massad's warehouse at year-end, what adjustment do you need to make and why?

I'd strip out the unrealised intercompany margin still embedded in closing stock — debit consolidated cost of sales, credit consolidated inventory, for the full markup amount, since no sale has actually occurred outside the group. Leaving it in would overstate both group profit and group inventory value simultaneously, which matters operationally too: if Massad's warehouse team is reporting inventory turns or margin based on the inflated transfer price rather than original third-party cost, every KPI built on top of that number is distorted. This is exactly the kind of control gap that's easy to miss if intercompany trading between Massad and Raad isn't tracked at SKU level with a clear cost-versus-transfer-price flag.

Q12Where does goodwill come from in a consolidation, and what do you do with it going forward — amortise it?

Goodwill arises at acquisition as the excess of consideration paid over the parent's share of the fair value of identifiable net assets acquired — it represents whatever the parent paid for synergies, brand value, customer relationships, and workforce that don't qualify as separately identifiable intangible assets under IFRS 3. Under current IFRS, goodwill is not amortised — it's carried at cost and tested for impairment at least annually, or more frequently if a triggering event occurs, such as a sustained downturn in one of the acquired businesses. For a 1995 and 2012 acquisition like Massad and Raad respectively, I'd want to confirm goodwill has actually been subjected to a documented annual impairment review historically, since this is one of the more commonly under-maintained areas in privately-held group accounts — it's easy for an impairment test to quietly lapse into a box-ticking exercise rather than a genuine cash-flow-based assessment.

Multi-Currency Intercompany Management

IAS 21.32

This is the single most commonly misunderstood area in multi-currency groups — including by people who can recite the consolidation steps perfectly. The core insight: an intercompany loan in a third currency does not automatically net to zero on consolidation.

Why the FX gain and loss don't cancel out

Picture the parent (KWD-functional) lending EUR 1,000,000 to Tazweed KSA (SAR-functional) to fund store buildout. Each entity retranslates this monetary balance at every reporting date — but using its own functional currency's rate against EUR. The parent uses EUR/KWD; the subsidiary uses EUR/SAR. These are two entirely different currency pairs moving for different underlying reasons (SAR is fixed to USD at a known peg; KWD floats against an undisclosed basket) — there is no mechanical reason their movements should offset.

IAS 21.32 — if the loan is, in substance, part of the parent's net investment in the foreign operation (long-term, not planned to be settled in the foreseeable future), the parent's own exchange difference is recognised in OCI instead of P&L, in the parent's separate financial statements, and only reclassified to the group P&L on disposal of the foreign operation.

Q13If the parent lends to a foreign subsidiary in a currency that's neither party's functional currency, why doesn't the FX gain or loss naturally net to zero on consolidation?

Because each entity is retranslating the same EUR balance through a different lens — the parent against EUR/KWD, the subsidiary against EUR/SAR — and those two currency pairs are driven by entirely separate factors. SAR is fixed to USD at a known peg, so EUR/SAR effectively just tracks EUR/USD; KWD floats against an undisclosed basket, so EUR/KWD moves differently again. There's no economic mechanism forcing those two movements to mirror each other, so the gain one entity books and the loss the other books are simply not designed to cancel out. The only piece that eliminates cleanly is the underlying loan principal itself when you match the intercompany receivable against the intercompany payable — the historical FX P&L impact each side already recognised in its own functional currency stays recognised, unless a specific reconciliation policy is built to address it.

Q14When does an intercompany loan's FX gain or loss go to OCI instead of the P&L?

Under IAS 21.32, if the intercompany loan is, in substance, part of the parent's net investment in a foreign operation — meaning it's long-term financing with no realistic intention or plan to be settled in the foreseeable future, functioning more like permanent capital than a short-term receivable — the parent's exchange difference on that balance is recognised in OCI in the parent's own separate financial statements, not the P&L. It only gets reclassified out of OCI into the consolidated P&L on the day the group disposes of or loses control of that foreign operation. The practical test isn't the loan documentation alone — it's genuine commercial substance: is this realistically going to be repaid in the next year or two, or is it effectively permanent funding dressed up as a loan? That determination should be made and documented at the time the loan is advanced, not retrofitted at year-end.

Q15What practical policy would you put in place to reduce multi-currency intercompany loan complexity across the group?

I'd avoid denominating intercompany loans in a third currency wherever possible — lending Tazweed KSA in SAR directly, rather than EUR, removes one entire leg of translation complexity since the subsidiary's liability then matches its own functional currency exactly. Where a third currency is unavoidable, I'd centralise FX risk at a single treasury function rather than let it sit split across multiple entities' books, and build a monthly intercompany reconciliation process specifically for FX-denominated balances so any mismatch between what each side has booked gets caught and resolved before it compounds across periods. I'd also document, at the point each intercompany loan is advanced, whether it's genuinely short-term or net-investment-in-substance under IAS 21.32 — that classification decision drives where the volatility lands, and it's far easier to get right at inception than to argue retroactively at year-end audit.

Q16Walk me through what gets eliminated on consolidation for an intercompany loan, and what doesn't.

What eliminates cleanly: the loan principal itself — debit the intercompany payable on the subsidiary's books, credit the intercompany receivable on the parent's books, removing both from the consolidated balance sheet entirely, since from the group's perspective it's just cash moved internally, not a real third-party liability. Any intercompany interest charged also eliminates — interest income on the parent's books against interest expense on the subsidiary's books cancels out in full. What does not automatically eliminate: the FX gains and losses each entity already recognised in its own functional-currency P&L during the year on that same balance, because they were computed against different currency pairs as we walked through above. Those require a specific FX elimination or reconciliation entry, not a default assumption that they'll wash out on their own — and if the loan is classified as net-investment-in-substance under IAS 21.32, the parent's own gain or loss sits in OCI rather than P&L in the first place, adding a further layer that needs explicit handling, not a generic intercompany elimination template.

Group Reporting & Segment Reporting

IFRS 8

The mechanics of consolidation are only useful if they feed a reporting structure leadership actually reads and acts on — this is where technical accuracy meets commercial usefulness.

Defining reportable segments

IFRS 8.5 defines an operating segment as a component whose results are regularly reviewed by the chief operating decision maker (here, realistically the Group CEO and GCFO together) to assess performance and allocate resources, with discrete financial information available for it. A segment is separately reportable once its revenue, profit/loss, or assets reach 10% or more of the relevant combined total (IFRS 8.13).

For Al Surouh Holding, the natural cut is FMCG (Massad + Raad) versus F&B (Tazweed) — but the real test isn't "what makes intuitive sense," it's what's actually reviewed as a discrete unit by the CEO and GCFO today. If Massad and Raad are reviewed and resourced separately rather than as one combined FMCG view, IFRS 8 would point toward three segments, not two.

Group reporting calendar

A workable monthly close-to-board-pack calendar for a multi-entity group, built around the GCC Sunday–Thursday working week:

Day 1–3: entity-level trial balance finalisation at Massad, Raad, and Tazweed individually.
Day 4–5: intercompany reconciliation and elimination — confirm balances agree before consolidation, not after.
Day 6: consolidation entries and group reporting pack assembly.
Day 7: management review and variance commentary.
Day 8–10: board pack finalisation and distribution.

What goes on the first page of the dashboard

For an FMCG/F&B group specifically, not a generic KPI list: gross margin % by brand or category, A&P spend as a percentage of net sales (FMCG benchmark typically 5–15% depending on category maturity), trade spend as a percentage of gross sales, the cash conversion cycle (DSO + DIO − DPO), and — specific to Tazweed — same-store sales growth, the standard F&B retail metric for distinguishing genuine growth from store-count growth.

Q17How would you define reportable segments for this group under IFRS 8 — would FMCG and F&B be one segment or two?

The standard's test isn't intuitive grouping — it's what's actually reviewed as a discrete unit by the chief operating decision maker. If the CEO and GCFO genuinely review Massad and Raad together as a combined FMCG view for resource allocation, that supports treating them as one segment alongside Tazweed as a second, F&B segment. But if the FMCG CEO actually reviews Massad and Raad's performance separately — different brand portfolios, different supplier relationships, potentially different growth trajectories — then IFRS 8's aggregation criteria would need testing properly before combining them, and the honest answer might be three reportable segments, not two. I wouldn't assume the intuitive grouping is the technically correct one without first confirming how performance is actually reviewed internally today.

Q18Walk me through your ideal monthly group reporting calendar from trial balance close to board pack.

I'd run it as a structured ten-working-day cycle aligned to the Sunday-Thursday week: the first three days close each entity's trial balance independently — Massad, Raad, and Tazweed each finalised on their own books before anything touches consolidation. Days four and five are dedicated specifically to intercompany reconciliation, confirming both sides of every intercompany balance agree before I even attempt to eliminate them — trying to consolidate before intercompany agrees is the single most common cause of a late, error-prone close. Day six is consolidation itself and assembling the group pack. Day seven is management review with the FMCG CEO and GCFO, where variance commentary gets challenged and refined before anyone outside finance sees it. The final two to three days finalise the board pack. The discipline that actually matters isn't the calendar itself — it's refusing to let consolidation start before intercompany genuinely reconciles.

Q19What KPIs would you put on the first page of the group dashboard, and why those specifically?

Gross margin percentage by brand, because raw revenue growth means nothing if it's coming from low-margin categories cannibalising better ones. A&P spend as a percentage of net sales, benchmarked against the 5-15% FMCG norm, so leadership can see immediately if marketing investment is under- or over-indexed relative to peers. Trade spend as a percentage of gross sales, tracked separately from A&P, because that's the line most prone to silent margin erosion if it isn't watched monthly. Cash conversion cycle, because a distribution business can look profitable on paper while quietly starving for working capital. And for Tazweed specifically, same-store sales growth rather than total revenue growth, since with an active store rollout underway, total revenue will grow almost regardless of underlying store performance — same-store growth is the only number that tells you if the concept itself is actually working.

Q20How do you handle a situation where local statutory accounts use a different chart of accounts than group reporting requires?

I build a single group chart of accounts with a mapping table against each entity's local statutory COA, so every entity files its required statutory accounts without compromise, while a consistent mapping layer rolls everything up into one harmonised group structure automatically rather than through manual monthly reclassification. This is hands-on territory for me — I recently rebuilt a multi-entity client's chart of accounts from roughly 400 ungoverned, overlapping accounts into a clean 465-account schema with explicit account ownership, specifically to enable this kind of instant roll-up without a manual mapping exercise every close. The mapping table itself needs version control and a documented change process — the most common failure mode isn't building the mapping once, it's letting it silently drift out of sync as local entities add accounts over time without updating the group-level map.

A&P / Trade Spend — Deep Dive

IFRS 15

The conceptual answer ("trade spend reduces revenue") is table stakes. The real test is whether you can actually journal it through from accrual to true-up to settlement.

The variable consideration constraint

IFRS 15.56-58 requires that variable consideration only be included in the transaction price to the extent it's highly probable that a significant reversal of cumulative revenue won't occur once the uncertainty resolves. Two estimation methods under IFRS 15.53: expected value — a probability-weighted average, better suited to a large number of similar promotions where you have a reliable historical pattern; and most likely amount, better suited to a binary outcome, like a volume rebate that's either achieved or it isn't.

Two categories of payment to a customer — and they're accounted for completely differently

Payment for no distinct good or service received in return — volume rebates, off-invoice discounts, listing/slotting fees paid purely for shelf space — reduces the transaction price directly (contra-revenue).

Payment in exchange for a genuinely distinct good or service — the retailer provides actual in-store marketing or advertising with evidence of performance — is accounted for as a purchased service, i.e. an A&P operating expense, but only up to the fair value of the service received. Any amount paid in excess of that fair value is still treated as a price reduction, not an expense.

At quarter-end, once actual cumulative volume is known, I'd true up the accrual to the confirmed amount — in the worked example, an additional KWD 4,500 to bring the running accrual from 38,000 to the confirmed 42,500. When the credit note is actually issued or cash paid, that simply clears the accrued liability against receivables or cash — no further P&L impact at that point, because the expense was already recognised progressively, not at settlement.

Q22A retailer asks Massad to pay for an in-store endcap display. Is that a marketing expense or a reduction of revenue — how do you decide?

The decision hinges on whether Massad is receiving a genuinely distinct good or service in return, with evidence of fair value. If the retailer can demonstrate the endcap placement has identifiable, standalone market value — comparable rate cards or third-party pricing for similar in-store media — and the payment doesn't exceed that fair value, it's a purchased service and belongs in A&P as an operating expense. If there's no real distinct service, or the payment is effectively just buying shelf space dressed up as a "service fee," or the amount paid exceeds the demonstrable fair value of what's received, the excess — or the whole amount, if there's no genuine distinct service at all — is treated as a reduction of revenue instead. In practice, I'd want a documented fair-value basis on file for every below-the-line payment classified as A&P rather than contra-revenue, because that distinction directly affects reported gross and net revenue, and it's exactly the kind of judgment auditors test closely.

Q23What's the difference between the expected value method and the most likely amount method, and which would you use for a volume rebate?

Expected value is a probability-weighted average across a range of possible outcomes — appropriate when there's a large population of similar contracts or promotions and you have reliable historical data to weight scenarios credibly, like a chain-wide promotional discount applied across hundreds of small transactions. Most likely amount is appropriate for a single, largely binary outcome — like a quarterly volume threshold that's either hit or it isn't, with no meaningful middle ground. For the back-end volume rebate example above, the threshold is genuinely binary — the retailer either crosses KWD 800,000 or doesn't — so most likely amount, based on the single most probable outcome given historical purchasing pattern, is the more defensible method. I'd reserve expected value for promotional programmes with a genuinely continuous range of redemption outcomes, like a coupon redemption rate across a large customer base.

Q24How do you build in the IFRS 15 constraint so you don't over-recognise revenue on a new promotional programme with no historical data?

Without historical redemption data, "highly probable no significant reversal" is genuinely difficult to support, so I'd default to the most conservative end of any reasonable estimate range until at least one or two redemption cycles establish an actual pattern — better to under-recognise net revenue early and true up upward later than the reverse, which risks a revenue restatement. Practically, I'd flag any brand-new promotional mechanic to the GCFO before launch specifically because of this constraint, agree a conservative initial accrual rate in writing, and set an explicit review trigger — say, after the first full redemption cycle — to recalibrate the rate based on real data rather than letting an initial guess quietly persist for multiple quarters unchallenged.

A1

Net Exposure Across Multiple Currencies

Treasury Policy
Plain English

Don't hedge every individual invoice — hedge the net position. If one part of the group owes euros and another part is owed euros, those two largely cancel each other out on their own. Hedging both separately is like buying insurance on money you don't actually stand to lose — it costs real money for protection you don't need.

Why centralised treasury matters more than entity-level hedging

Each operating entity, left to manage its own banking relationships independently, only sees its own slice of the group's currency exposure. A group treasury function sees the whole picture across entities and currencies, and nets exposures before going to the market — reducing both the notional amount that needs hedging and the transaction costs (spreads, forward points) paid on each contract.

Worked Example — Group-Level EUR Netting

Massad owes EUR 1,200,000 to its dairy suppliers, due in 90 days. Separately, and entirely unrelated to Massad's payable, Raad is owed EUR 350,000 — a volume-growth rebate from a European water brand's parent company, due in roughly the same window.

Why it matters: if Massad and Raad each banked independently, Massad would likely hedge its full EUR 1,200,000 payable without ever knowing Raad's EUR 350,000 receivable existed — the group would carry an unnecessary EUR 350,000 of hedging notional and pay forward-contract costs on money it didn't need to protect. A centralised treasury function spots this and only needs to hedge the true net EUR 850,000 group exposure.
Q1Why would you net exposures across two different legal entities before hedging, rather than letting each entity manage its own FX independently?

Because the group's true economic exposure is the net position, not the sum of every entity's gross position. If Massad's euro payable and Raad's euro receivable are left to be managed separately, the group ends up paying for hedging protection on an exposure that, at the consolidated level, barely exists — Raad's receivable already offsets most of Massad's payable economically, even though they sit on different entities' books. Centralising the view doesn't change either entity's standalone accounting treatment, but it changes how much the group actually needs to transact in the forward market, directly reducing cost.

Q2Does netting exposures across entities for hedging purposes mean Massad and Raad's individual books also show a netted FX position?

No — netting for treasury and hedging decisions is a group-level economic exercise, not an accounting one. Massad's books still carry its full EUR 1,200,000 payable, retranslated and accounted for on its own standalone financial statements exactly as it would be in isolation, and Raad's books still carry its own EUR 350,000 receivable separately. The netting only changes how much notional the group's treasury function actually transacts with a bank to hedge — typically through one entity (often the parent or a designated treasury entity) entering a forward for the net amount, then internal allocation or intercompany arrangements handling how that hedge benefit is reflected back to the individual entities, which itself needs a clear, documented intercompany policy to avoid disputes over which entity "owns" the hedge gain or loss.

Hedge Effectiveness & Basis Risk

IFRS 9
Plain English

Hedging with the wrong currency pair is like buying travel insurance for a trip to Dubai when you're actually flying to London — close, related, but not exact, and the gap between "close" and "exact" is where you still get hurt. If your bank can't quote you EUR/KWD directly and you have to build the hedge in two steps through USD, each step has its own small cost and timing risk, and those add up.

Why a "perfect" hedge often isn't, in the Kuwait market

Because KWD is pegged to an undisclosed basket rather than traded as a major currency, local banks often don't offer a deep, liquid EUR/KWD forward market the way they do for USD/KWD. In practice, hedging a EUR exposure can mean executing two legs — KWD against USD, then USD against EUR — instead of one direct EUR/KWD contract. Each leg carries its own bid/offer spread, and the two legs may not settle on identical dates, creating cross-currency basis risk: the realised effective rate can differ from the rate that was intended, even though both legs were individually "hedged."

Worked Example — Cost of a Synthetic (Two-Leg) Hedge

Massad needs to hedge the EUR 850,000 net exposure from A1, settling in 90 days.

Why it matters: KWD 1,275 looks small on one transaction, but it's a real, recurring cost on every EUR hedge the group executes if a direct quote isn't available — and it's exactly the kind of cost that gets missed if treasury policy only looks at "did we hedge, yes or no" rather than "what did the hedge actually cost us to execute." It's also why hedge effectiveness testing under IFRS 9 exists: if the basis risk between the hedging instrument and the underlying exposure is large enough, the hedge may not qualify as "highly effective," forcing fair value movements to hit the P&L directly through profit or loss rather than being deferred in OCI.
Q3Why might a EUR hedge for a Kuwaiti company not be as clean as a USD hedge, even though both are "major" currencies?

KWD's peg to an undisclosed currency basket means local banks have deep, liquid pricing for KWD against USD specifically, but EUR/KWD quotes are often thinner or unavailable at the size and tenor a corporate needs, forcing a synthetic two-leg structure through USD instead of one clean direct forward. Each additional leg introduces its own spread cost and its own settlement-timing risk, and those two legs don't move in perfect lockstep with each other — that gap between the synthetic effective rate and what a direct quote would have offered is basis risk, and it's a real, measurable cost, not just a theoretical concern.

Q4What happens, from an accounting perspective, if a hedge isn't considered "highly effective" under IFRS 9?

If the hedge relationship doesn't meet IFRS 9's effectiveness requirements — broadly, an economic relationship between the hedged item and hedging instrument that isn't dominated by credit risk, with a hedge ratio reflecting the actual quantities used — hedge accounting can't be applied, or breaks down where it previously qualified. Practically, that means the forward contract's fair value movements get recognised straight through profit or loss as they occur, rather than being deferred in OCI until the underlying transaction happens. That reintroduces exactly the P&L volatility hedge accounting was designed to avoid, and it's a strong reason to document hedge designation, hedge ratio, and the basis-risk assessment properly at inception rather than treating hedge accounting as automatic just because a forward contract exists.

A3

Net Investment Hedging — Hedging the Translation Loss Itself

IAS 21.32 · IFRS 9
Plain English

If you own a house priced in a foreign currency, and you take out the mortgage on that same house in that same foreign currency, the two move together — if the currency drops, both the house's value in your home currency and your mortgage's value in your home currency drop by roughly the same amount, and they cancel out. Borrow in your own currency instead, and only the house value moves — you're fully exposed with nothing offsetting it.

The mechanic: funding currency as a hedge

If the group funds a foreign subsidiary's expansion using debt borrowed in that subsidiary's own functional currency — rather than lending KWD or EUR from the parent — and formally designates that borrowing as a hedge of the net investment, IAS 21.32 (read with IFRS 9's hedge accounting rules) allows the FX gain or loss on the borrowing to be recognised in OCI, in the same translation reserve as the CTA on the subsidiary itself. The two move in opposite directions and largely cancel out — exactly because the loan and the net investment are both denominated in, and therefore both swing with, the same foreign currency.

Q5How would you reduce the translation volatility that Tazweed KSA's expansion creates on the group's reported equity, without using a derivative?

Fund the expansion with SAR-denominated borrowing rather than lending KWD or EUR from the parent, and formally designate that borrowing as a hedge of the net investment under IAS 21.32. Because the loan and the net investment are both denominated in SAR, they both move in the same direction relative to KWD as the exchange rate shifts — when the net investment's translated KWD value falls because SAR has weakened, the SAR loan's translated KWD liability value falls by roughly the same amount, and the two movements offset each other directly in OCI rather than creating two separate, uncorrelated swings in group equity. No derivative instrument is needed — the foreign-currency-denominated debt itself is the hedging instrument.

Q6If the group only partially funds the SAR expansion with SAR debt and the rest from KWD equity, what happens to the unhedged portion?

The unhedged portion of the net investment still generates its own translation gain or loss in OCI, with nothing offsetting it — in the worked example, borrowing SAR 2,500,000 against a SAR 3,000,000 net investment left roughly one-sixth of the position unhedged, and that unhedged sixth produced a residual KWD 1,345 net loss to OCI even after the hedge gain on the SAR loan was applied. The lesson for hedge design isn't just "hedge or don't" — it's that the hedge ratio needs to be sized deliberately against the actual net investment, and if the group only wants partial protection, that residual exposure should be a conscious, documented decision rather than an accidental gap discovered at year-end.

Q7Why does the gain on the SAR loan go to OCI instead of the P&L, given it's still just an FX gain on a monetary liability?

Normally, yes — a gain or loss on a foreign-currency monetary liability goes straight to P&L under IAS 21.28. The exception here is that this specific borrowing has been formally designated, under IFRS 9's hedge accounting rules, as a hedge of the net investment in a foreign operation. That designation is what redirects the effective portion of the gain into OCI, into the same translation reserve as the CTA it's offsetting, rather than the P&L — without that formal designation and documentation at inception, the loan would simply be treated as ordinary foreign-currency debt, and its FX movement would hit the income statement directly, creating a mismatch against the CTA sitting quietly in equity rather than the clean offset shown in the worked example. The accounting treatment exists specifically to make the financial statements reflect the genuine economic hedge relationship, not to be a default outcome of simply borrowing in a foreign currency.

B1

Multi-Tier Group Structures & Indirect NCI

IFRS 10
Plain English

Ownership multiplies down a chain, like a family inheritance passed through generations — your real stake in your grandchild company is your stake in your child, multiplied by your child's stake in the grandchild. And the leftover ownership — the part you don't effectively hold — can come from more than one source at once.

Effective interest cascades; control doesn't dilute

If the parent controls an intermediate subsidiary, and that subsidiary in turn controls a further entity beneath it, the parent controls the entire chain — and IFRS 10 requires 100% of that bottom entity's assets, liabilities, income, and expenses to be consolidated line by line into the group accounts, regardless of how diluted the parent's effective ownership percentage has become. What changes as you go down the chain isn't whether you consolidate — it's how large the Non-Controlling Interest is, and where it comes from.

Worked Example — Three-Tier Ownership (Illustrative)

Parent owns 80% of Massad. Massad, in turn, owns 75% of a hypothetical specialty cold-chain logistics entity, "ColdLink Co," with the remaining 25% held directly by an external logistics partner.

Why it matters: the 40% NCI shown against ColdLink Co on the consolidated balance sheet isn't one homogenous block — 15 points of it belongs to Massad's own minority shareholders, who never directly invested in ColdLink at all, and 25 points belongs to a genuinely separate external party who did. Both still get consolidated together as "Non-Controlling Interest" on the group balance sheet, but understanding the split matters when, for instance, ColdLink's external partner wants to be bought out — that negotiation only concerns the 25%, not the full 40%.
Q8If Massad only owns 75% of a sub-subsidiary, and the parent only owns 80% of Massad, do you consolidate 100% of that sub-subsidiary, or just the parent's effective 60%?

100%, in full — control, not effective ownership percentage, is what drives consolidation under IFRS 10. The parent controls Massad, and Massad controls the sub-subsidiary, so control cascades all the way down the chain even though the parent's effective economic interest has been diluted to 60% by the two ownership layers. What the 60% effective interest actually drives is how much of the sub-subsidiary's profit gets attributed to "owners of the parent" on the consolidated P&L versus how much sits in the NCI line — not whether the entity gets consolidated at all.

Q9Why does the Non-Controlling Interest in a sub-subsidiary sometimes come from two different sources, and does that matter practically?

It happens whenever there's a minority stake at more than one level of the ownership chain — here, Massad itself has a 20% external minority, and ColdLink Co has its own separate 25% external partner, so ColdLink's total 40% NCI is really two distinct groups of outside shareholders combined into one consolidated balance sheet line. It matters practically for two reasons: first, dividend flows and any future buyout negotiation need to be tracked against the correct underlying party, not a blended number; and second, if either minority stake is ever revalued, sold, or renegotiated, the consolidation entries need to isolate which portion of the 40% is actually changing, rather than treating the NCI balance as one undifferentiated block.

Mid-Year Acquisitions — The Partial-Period Trap

IFRS 3
Plain English

You only consolidate the slice of time you actually owned the business — like only paying rent for the days you actually lived somewhere, not the whole month. Include results from before you owned it, and you've quietly inflated the group's growth story.

Only post-acquisition results enter consolidated figures

A target acquired partway through the fiscal year contributes to consolidated revenue and profit only from the acquisition date forward. Pre-acquisition results belonged to the previous owner and never enter the group's consolidated P&L at all — not even as a comparative adjustment. The acquired entity's opening balance sheet is also fair-valued as of the acquisition date specifically (IFRS 3's purchase price allocation), not the start of the fiscal year, so any fair-value step-ups to inventory or newly recognised intangibles only start affecting the P&L from that same date forward.

Worked Example — Six-Month Contribution, Not Twelve

A target is acquired on 1 July, six months into the fiscal year. Full-year results (assuming even performance through the year, for simplicity): Revenue KWD 2,000,000, Net Profit KWD 300,000.

PeriodRevenueNet ProfitIncluded in group consolidation?
1 Jan – 30 Jun (pre-acquisition)1,000,000150,000No
1 Jul – 31 Dec (post-acquisition)1,000,000150,000Yes
Why it matters: including the full KWD 2,000,000 / KWD 300,000 — a genuinely common preparer error, whether through carelessness or to flatter reported growth — overstates group revenue and profit growth and misleads the board about whether performance is organic or acquisitive. Board commentary on any acquisition-year results should explicitly state the number of months of contribution and the acquisition date, every time, so growth variance analysis isn't quietly distorted by an unstated mid-year acquisition.
Q10If a target is acquired on 1 July, why wouldn't you just include its full-year results since you're consolidating it for the annual report anyway?

Because the group never controlled that entity for the first six months — those results belong economically to the previous owner, who bore the risk and got the reward of that period's performance. Including the full year would mean reporting profit the group never actually earned or had any influence over, which misstates both the income statement and any growth metrics derived from it. Consolidation reflects the period of control, not the calendar year an acquisition happens to fall within — exactly the same logic as not paying rent for days you didn't live somewhere.

Q11Beyond the P&L cutoff, what else changes because the acquisition happened mid-year rather than at the start of the fiscal year?

The purchase price allocation under IFRS 3 — fair-valuing the target's identifiable assets and liabilities, recognising any new intangibles like customer relationships, and calculating goodwill — is all performed as of the actual acquisition date, 1 July, not 1 January. That means any fair-value step-up to inventory, for instance, only flows through cost of sales from 1 July forward as that stepped-up inventory is sold, and any newly recognised intangible only starts amortising from 1 July. If someone mistakenly back-dated the fair value exercise to the start of the year, both the balance sheet at acquisition and the subsequent amortisation or depreciation schedule would be wrong, compounding the mid-year-inclusion error rather than just stopping at the P&L cutoff point.

Step Acquisitions — From Associate to Subsidiary

IFRS 3.42
Plain English

If you already own a slice of a cake and then buy enough additional slices to own most of it, you don't just add your old slice's original price to what you paid for the new ones — the rules make you re-price your original slice at today's value first, and recognise whatever it gained or lost in value since you first bought it.

Remeasurement of the previously-held interest

When a parent already holds a minority, equity-accounted interest in a business and then acquires enough additional shares to obtain control, IFRS 3.42 requires the previously-held interest to be remeasured to its fair value at the date control is obtained, with any resulting gain or loss recognised in profit or loss — not simply carried forward at its old equity-method book value. That remeasured fair value, plus the cash paid for the new shares, together form the total deemed consideration used to calculate goodwill on the now-fully-consolidated subsidiary.

Worked Example — 30% Associate Becomes 80% Subsidiary

Parent has held a 30% associate stake for several years, carried under the equity method at KWD 180,000. It now acquires an additional 50% for KWD 350,000 cash, taking total ownership to 80% and obtaining control.

That skips the required KWD 30,000 P&L gain entirely and understates total consideration used for the goodwill calculation, producing both a wrong P&L and a wrong goodwill figure in the same transaction.
Q12Why does buying additional shares to gain control trigger a gain on shares the company already owned and isn't selling?

Because obtaining control is treated as a significant change in the nature of the relationship — moving from "significant influence" under the equity method to genuine control and full consolidation — and IFRS 3 treats that transition as if the previously-held interest were, in substance, disposed of at fair value and immediately reacquired as part of the new controlling stake. The previously-held 30% had been sitting on the books at its historical equity-method carrying value, which doesn't necessarily reflect what that stake is actually worth today; the price paid for the new 50% gives a real, market-based signal of current fair value, and the gap between that implied fair value and the old book value is recognised as a genuine economic gain, even though no shares were physically sold.

Q13How do you determine the fair value of the previously-held interest if you don't have an independent valuation?

The price paid for the new controlling tranche is the strongest available evidence of fair value, provided it was negotiated at arm's length — scaling that price up to an implied 100% value and then applying it to the previously-held percentage, exactly as in the worked example, is the standard practical approach when a fresh, observable transaction has just occurred. Where the transaction price isn't a clean arm's-length indicator — say, a related-party step-up, or a price influenced by factors specific to the incremental control premium rather than the per-share value generally — a more formal valuation exercise becomes necessary, since simply scaling the transaction price could overstate or understate the previously-held interest's fair value. I'd flag that judgment call explicitly to the GCFO rather than assume the transaction price is automatically the right answer in every case.

Q14What's the most common mistake people make when accounting for a step acquisition like this?

Simply adding the old equity-method book value of the previously-held stake to the cash paid for the new shares, and treating that sum as total consideration — skipping the required fair-value remeasurement of the previously-held interest entirely. That error understates both the P&L gain that should be recognised on the deemed disposal-and-reacquisition of the old stake, and understates total consideration used in the goodwill calculation, since the previously-held interest is being carried in at its stale historical cost rather than its current fair value. It's an easy mistake to make because intuitively it feels like you're just "adding to an existing investment," when IFRS 3 actually treats the moment control is obtained as a clean break requiring a fresh fair-value assessment of everything the acquirer now holds, not just the newly purchased portion.

B4

Two-Step Translation — Local ≠ Functional ≠ Presentation Currency

IAS 21.23
Plain English

Sometimes a business keeps its day-to-day books in one currency purely out of local habit or regulation, but its real economic currency — the one that actually drives its prices and costs — is a different one entirely. You have to translate its books into its true economic currency first, before you can then translate that into the group's reporting currency. Two separate translations, two completely different methods, because they're solving two different problems.

Step 1 — Remeasurement (local currency → functional currency)

IAS 21.23 sets out the "monetary/non-monetary method" for this first step, which is fundamentally different from the current-rate method used in Volume 2: monetary items (cash, receivables, payables) are remeasured at the closing rate at each reporting date, with the resulting gain or loss flowing straight to profit or loss. Non-monetary items carried at historical cost (inventory, fixed assets) are remeasured at the historical rate ruling when each item was originally acquired, and stay frozen at that functional-currency value regardless of how the local currency subsequently moves.

Step 2, once everything sits in functional currency, is the familiar current-rate method from Volume 2 — translate to presentation currency, with the resulting difference going to OCI as a CTA, not the P&L.

Q15Why would an entity's functional currency ever be different from the currency it actually keeps its statutory books in?

Statutory bookkeeping currency is usually a regulatory or administrative requirement — the local company registrar typically expects accounts in the local currency, regardless of how the business actually operates economically. Functional currency, under IAS 21, is purely a question of fact about the business itself: which currency genuinely drives its sales pricing and which currency drives its costs. A sourcing or trading entity whose contracts, invoicing, and supplier pricing are all denominated in a hard currency like USD — even while its local bookkeeping happens to be kept in the local currency for compliance — has a USD functional currency in substance, and the accounting has to reflect that economic reality through remeasurement, not just follow whatever currency the local ledger happens to be maintained in.

Q16Why does the cash remeasurement loss go to P&L, while the equivalent CTA on a foreign subsidiary's net assets goes to OCI — aren't they both just FX losses from a weakening local currency?

They look similar on the surface but represent fundamentally different things conceptually. Remeasurement under IAS 21.23 is treated as if the entity had been transacting directly in its functional currency all along — the local currency is essentially a bookkeeping convenience, and the remeasurement loss represents a real economic loss in the entity's true operating currency, which is exactly why it flows through P&L immediately, the same as any other monetary FX loss. Translation under the current-rate method, by contrast, applies when an entity's functional currency itself is genuinely different from the group's presentation currency — the entity is operating perfectly normally in its own functional currency with no real economic loss to that entity at all; the CTA only arises because the group chooses to present its consolidated results in a different currency than the subsidiary actually operates in, which is a presentation question, not an operating loss, and that's precisely why it sits in OCI rather than the P&L.

Q17If the local currency in this example kept weakening every year, would the inventory's functional-currency value ever change?

Not from the exchange rate alone — that's the entire point of the non-monetary, historical-rate treatment. The inventory's USD-functional value was fixed the moment it was acquired, at whatever the local-currency-to-USD rate was on that purchase date, and it stays at that USD value on the books regardless of how far the local currency subsequently falls, right up until the inventory is actually sold, consumed, or written down for a genuine impairment reason unrelated to FX. The only way that USD-equivalent value would change is through a normal lower-of-cost-or-net-realisable-value write-down under IAS 2, applied in the functional currency, completely separate from any further currency remeasurement. This is precisely why monetary and non-monetary items have to be tracked distinctly in this kind of entity — treating inventory like cash and re-translating it at every closing rate would manufacture a fictitious, recurring FX loss that doesn't reflect anything real happening to the business.

Equity Method vs Full Consolidation

IAS 28 · IFRS 11
Plain English

Full consolidation is like merging your finances completely with someone you control — you report every dollar that flows through, even the slice that isn't really "yours." The equity method is like just tracking your share of someone else's profit growth on a single line, because you have influence over them but you don't get to dictate what they do with the cash.

One line versus the whole P&L

Where the parent has control (typically >50%, or control through other rights even at a lower percentage), full consolidation applies — every revenue, cost, asset, and liability line is combined 100%, with NCI absorbing the unowned share. Where the parent has only significant influence (typically 20-49%) without control — common for a strategic minority stake or a genuine joint venture brand-distribution arrangement — the equity method applies instead: a single "Investment in Associate/JV" line on the balance sheet, with the investor's share of the investee's profit recognised through one P&L line, not spread across the investor's own revenue, COGS, and expense lines.

Worked Example — Equity-Accounting a 35% JV Stake

Parent holds 35% of a joint-venture distributor. The JV earns KWD 400,000 profit for the year and later distributes a KWD 100,000 dividend.

Why it matters: the dividend entry reduces the investment balance rather than hitting the P&L again — the profit was already recognised in full when it was earned, not when it was eventually distributed as cash, so booking the dividend as income too would double-count it. The investment carrying value simply builds up with the investor's share of retained profit and runs back down as cash is actually distributed.
Q18If the group entered a 50/50 joint venture to bring a new brand into Kuwait, how would that affect group reported revenue compared to a wholly-owned distribution arm?

Materially less revenue would show on the group's consolidated top line, even if the JV itself generates substantial sales. A wholly-owned entity's full revenue gets combined line by line into group revenue; a 50/50 joint venture under the equity method contributes only the group's 50% share of the JV's net profit as a single line item — the JV's gross revenue never appears in consolidated revenue at all. That's an important distinction to flag proactively to leadership before structuring a new brand partnership as a JV rather than a wholly-owned subsidiary, since a JV structure that makes commercial sense — shared risk, a partner's local relationships — can look like it's "shrinking" reported group revenue purely because of how the accounting standard requires it to be presented, not because the underlying business performed any differently.

Q19What's the practical trigger that moves an investment from equity method to full consolidation, beyond just crossing 50% ownership?

Ownership percentage is a useful proxy but isn't the actual test under IFRS 10 — the real question is whether the investor has obtained control: power over the investee's relevant activities, exposure to variable returns, and the ability to use that power to affect those returns. That can happen below 50% if the investor holds disproportionate board representation, veto or casting-vote rights over key decisions, or effective control through other shareholders' apathy in a widely-held entity — and conversely, a stake above 50% might not constitute control if genuine substantive participating rights are held by other parties that block the majority owner's unilateral decision-making. I'd want the actual shareholders' agreement and board governance terms reviewed for any borderline stake, rather than assuming the percentage alone settles the consolidation question.

F&B vs FMCG — The Business Model Shift

Plain English

FMCG sells through people — Massad and Raad hand goods to a retailer, who then sells to the actual end customer. F&B sells directly to people — Tazweed is the last link in the chain, the till that takes the customer's money. That one structural difference changes almost everything else about how the business needs to be managed financially.

Why this isn't just "a different product," it's a different business

In FMCG distribution, the customer is a retailer — a business that pays on credit terms, negotiates trade spend, and resells the product later. The whole finance toolkit you already run at Massad and Raad — accounts receivable discipline, rebate accruals, sell-in revenue recognition — exists because of that intermediary relationship. In F&B, there is no intermediary. The customer pays cash or card at the point of sale, the transaction is complete in seconds, and there's no resale step at all. That single change removes an entire category of risk you currently manage daily (credit risk, collections, rebate negotiation) and replaces it with a completely different set: labour cost control, food safety and wastage, and — critically — whether the physical location itself is any good.

The Store as the Unit of Analysis

Plain English

In FMCG, the company is the business. In F&B, every single store is its own little business, and has to work as one on its own terms — a strong company-wide P&L can hide three brilliant stores covering for two that are quietly losing money every month.

Four-wall economics

"Four walls" means the P&L of one specific store, stripped down to only what happens inside those four walls — revenue, cost of food and beverage sold, labour, and occupancy (rent and related costs) — before any head-office overhead gets allocated to it. This is deliberate: head-office costs don't scale per store, so blending them in early hides whether the store itself is actually a good business. Four-wall EBITDA is the single cleanest number for judging whether a specific location works.

Distribution labour costs barely move month to month by comparison — F&B labour cost is something you actively manage on a near-daily scheduling basis, not something you simply budget once a year.

Store Investment Economics — Capex & Payback

Plain English

Every new store is a mini-investment with its own clock running. You spend a lump sum up front to build it, then wait for that store's own profit to earn the money back — the question that actually matters for evaluating a multi-store rollout is simply: how long does that clock take to hit zero?

Capex components and the payback formula

Opening a new store requires a one-off capital outlay covering fit-out and interior design (especially significant for a premium, design-led concept), kitchen and beverage equipment, leasehold improvements to the rented space, and pre-opening costs like staff training and initial marketing. Payback period is simply that total capex divided by the store's stabilised annual four-wall EBITDA — it answers "how many years of this store's own profit does it take to earn back what we spent opening it."

Q5If you were asked to evaluate whether the pace of the Saudi expansion makes financial sense, what would you actually calculate?

Payback period per store — capex divided by stabilised four-wall EBITDA — compared against the group's cost of capital, and against how long it realistically takes before the cash thrown off by earlier stores can help fund the next one. If payback is stretching well beyond four to five years for a concept at this investment scale, that's worth flagging before committing to an aggressive multi-store rollout, not after the capital is already spent. I'd also want to know whether new stores are expected to perform at the same AUV and margin as the first ones, or whether the model assumes some ramp-up period — that assumption alone can swing the payback math significantly.

Q6Why might payback periods get worse, not better, as Tazweed opens more stores in the same market?

Two reasons worth flagging proactively. First, the best, highest-footfall locations typically get taken first — each subsequent store is more likely to land in a marginally weaker location than the one before it, all else equal. Second, cannibalization: a new store opened too close to an existing one doesn't necessarily create new sales, it can simply split existing demand between two locations, weakening the unit economics of both rather than adding a genuinely new profit stream. Modelling expansion pace without testing for cannibalization risk against the existing store map would overstate how much value each new opening actually adds.

GCC-Specific Dynamics — Ramadan, Real Estate, Labour

Plain English

The exact same café behaves completely differently depending on where it sits in the Gulf calendar year — Ramadan alone can flip a store's daily trading pattern upside down, and that's something a generic Western café financial model simply doesn't account for.

Ramadan seasonality

During Ramadan, daytime café traffic drops meaningfully as customers fast from dawn to sunset, while evening hours — particularly around iftar and again before dawn for suhoor — see a genuine spike in demand. Because Ramadan follows the lunar Islamic calendar, it shifts roughly ten to eleven days earlier each Gregorian year, meaning it lands in a different month every few years. Any annual budget, same-store-sales comparison, or monthly variance review has to adjust for exactly where Ramadan falls in each period being compared — comparing a month containing Ramadan against the same calendar month a year earlier, when Ramadan fell elsewhere entirely, produces a misleading variance unless it's explicitly normalised for.

GCC retail real estate and labour structure

Percentage or turnover rent — a base rent plus an additional percentage of sales above a threshold — is common practice in GCC mall leasing for F&B and retail tenants, meaning occupancy cost isn't always the fixed, predictable line item it would be in a simpler lease structure; it can move with the store's own sales performance. On labour, Kuwait's F&B staffing model typically relies heavily on expatriate labour under sponsorship arrangements, which carries its own cost structure — visa, accommodation, and turnover considerations — distinct from the labour cost assumptions embedded in a Western or even a different-GCC-market café model.

Franchise Fee Accounting

IFRS 15
Plain English

A franchise agreement typically has two completely separate payments bundled inside one relationship: a large one-time payment for the right to use the brand at all, and a smaller ongoing payment every month for as long as you keep using it. The accounting rule is simple — the one-time right is an asset, the monthly fee is an operating cost. Never mix them.

Initial franchise fee — an intangible asset, not an expense

The initial franchise fee paid to Lavazza for the right to operate the "Coffee Design Concept" in Kuwait and Saudi Arabia is capitalised as an intangible asset under IAS 38, specifically the right to operate under the Lavazza brand — not expensed immediately. It is then amortised on a straight-line basis over the term of the franchise agreement, typically aligned to the contractual licence period. If the agreement were for ten years, the KD cost is divided equally and expensed each year over those ten years — no more, no less.

The right must meet IAS 38's recognition criteria: identifiable, controlled by the entity, and probable future economic benefits. A master franchisee's exclusive regional rights clearly satisfy all three.

Ongoing royalty & marketing fund — operating expense as incurred

The ongoing royalty — typically a percentage of net sales charged monthly — is a performance obligation under IFRS 15 viewed from the franchisor's side, and simply an operating cost on Tazweed's books as incurred. It does not go through the balance sheet. The marketing fund contribution (a separate percentage of sales paid into a pooled brand marketing fund controlled by Lavazza) is similarly expensed as incurred — it is not Tazweed's asset, since Tazweed does not control how those pooled funds are spent.

Q1Why isn't the initial franchise fee expensed immediately when it's paid, the same way you'd expense a consulting invoice?

Because it's buying a future economic benefit that will generate revenue over multiple years — the right to use the Lavazza brand to sell coffee across Kuwait and Saudi Arabia for the term of the agreement. Expensing it immediately would front-load a real cost against revenue that hasn't been earned yet, violating the matching principle. IAS 38 requires it to be capitalised as an intangible asset and amortised over the period it actually benefits — the franchise term — because that's when Tazweed is actually consuming the value it paid for.

Q2What happens to the unamortised franchise fee balance if the franchise agreement is terminated early?

The remaining unamortised balance is written off immediately to P&L as an impairment loss in the period termination occurs — because the future economic benefit the intangible was supposed to deliver no longer exists. There's no spreading it out or deferring it; the asset is impaired in full the moment the right to use the brand ceases. This is worth flagging proactively as a risk in the franchise relationship: a deterioration in the Tazweed/Lavazza relationship would have a balance sheet consequence that's immediate and fully visible in the income statement, not just a business disruption.

Q3The marketing fund contribution goes to Lavazza, not to Tazweed's own marketing. Why is it still an expense on Tazweed's books?

Because Tazweed doesn't control the pooled fund — it doesn't decide how the money is spent, when it's spent, or on which markets. Control is the key test: an entity only recognises an asset when it controls the future economic benefit. Here, Lavazza controls the fund and directs the spend. So when Tazweed pays into the pool, it's transferring cash to a pool it has no authority over — that's an expense as incurred, not a prepaid asset or an investment. The practical implication is that Tazweed needs to negotiate clearly what minimum brand support it gets back in return for each KWD it puts into the pool, because once the money goes in, Tazweed has no claim to it as an asset on its own balance sheet.

A2

IFRS 16 — Store Leases

IFRS 16
Plain English

Before IFRS 16 (effective 2019), a company could rent a shop for five years and never show the obligation on its balance sheet — the full rent simply hit the P&L each month as an operating expense and that was it. IFRS 16 changed this: now you must treat every significant store lease as if you borrowed money to buy the right to occupy that space. The total future rent obligation goes onto the balance sheet as a liability, and a matching "right to use the space" goes on as an asset — the moment you sign the lease.

What IFRS 16 does to a multi-store F&B company's balance sheet

For a company like Tazweed with multiple stores, each lease generates a right-of-use (ROU) asset and a corresponding lease liability on day one. The lease liability is the present value of all future lease payments, discounted at either the rate implicit in the lease or — far more commonly — the lessee's incremental borrowing rate. The ROU asset equals the lease liability at inception, adjusted for any lease incentives received from the landlord (rent-free periods, fit-out contributions), prepayments, and initial direct costs.

Over the lease term: the ROU asset is depreciated on a straight-line basis to P&L. The lease liability is unwound like a loan — each month's payment splits between interest (finance cost to P&L) and principal repayment (reduces the liability). The rent line on the P&L disappears entirely and is replaced by two separate charges: depreciation and interest.

Short-term leases (under 12 months) and low-value leases may elect the practical expedient — expense as incurred, no balance sheet impact.

Q4Walk me through what IFRS 16 does to Tazweed's balance sheet versus what it looked like before IFRS 16 existed.

Before IFRS 16, every store lease was off-balance-sheet — rent hit the P&L monthly, nothing appeared on the balance sheet, and the full future rent obligation was invisible to anyone reading the accounts. Under IFRS 16, day one of each new store lease generates two new balance sheet items simultaneously: a right-of-use asset representing the right to use that store space for the lease term, and a matching lease liability representing the present value of all future payments. For Tazweed opening multiple stores across Kuwait and Saudi Arabia, this means the balance sheet progressively accumulates both a growing fixed asset base and a growing financial liability — total assets increase, but so does debt-equivalent leverage, and that matters for any banking covenant or group leverage ratio that's being monitored.

Q5A landlord offers Tazweed six months' rent-free as an incentive to sign a five-year lease. How does that change the Day 1 accounting?

The rent-free period is a lease incentive received — it reduces the initial measurement of the right-of-use asset rather than being recognised as income immediately. The lease liability is still based on the total undiscounted future payments due to the landlord across the full term (only the actual cash payments count, not the rent-free period), but the ROU asset is reduced by the value of the incentive at inception. The net effect is that the rent-free period gets spread across the entire lease term through the lower depreciation charge on the reduced ROU asset, rather than creating a one-off income boost in month one — which is exactly right economically, since the "free" period is really just a renegotiation of the total payment profile across the whole term.

Q6How would you determine the discount rate to use for a Tazweed lease in Kuwait where no rate is implicit in the lease document?

Use the incremental borrowing rate — the rate Tazweed would have to pay if it borrowed in the same currency, for the same term, and with the same security as the underlying leased asset. Practically in Kuwait, that means looking at what the group's existing bankers would charge on a KWD-denominated term loan of the same tenor as the lease, adjusted for the fact that a secured loan backed by the asset would typically attract a lower rate than an unsecured one. For a multi-store rollout, I'd establish a documented methodology approved by the GCFO for determining this rate at each lease commencement, rather than picking a number fresh each time — consistency of application matters for audit, and for comparability across the portfolio as it grows.

Gift Cards & Loyalty — The Liability You Don't See Coming

IFRS 15
Plain English

When a customer pays KWD 10 for a gift card, Tazweed has the cash but has done nothing yet — the customer hasn't had their coffee. That KWD 10 is a liability until the card is actually used. Not recognising that, or not tracking it properly across dozens of stores, is how cash balances look inflated for months until the cards get redeemed all at once.

Contract liability recognition — and breakage

Gift cards and loyalty points are treated as contract liabilities under IFRS 15 — the customer has paid, but the performance obligation (delivering the coffee) hasn't been fulfilled yet. Revenue is deferred until the card or points are actually redeemed. The complication unique to this area is breakage — gift cards that are never redeemed, loyalty points that expire. If the pattern shows statistically that, say, 8% of gift card balances are never redeemed, that 8% is recognised as breakage revenue — but only proportionally to the pattern of actual redemptions, not as a lump sum when the cards are first sold. This means a loyalty programme isn't just a marketing tool — it's a revenue recognition complexity that needs its own tracking system, not a spreadsheet.

Food Cost, Wastage & Inventory

IAS 2
Plain English

In FMCG you count boxes on a pallet. In F&B you count kilograms of milk and grams of coffee, with a shelf life measured in days. The challenge isn't that the inventory rules are different — IAS 2 applies to both — it's that the operational discipline needed to actually apply those rules in a working café kitchen is completely different from a warehouse environment.

IAS 2 in an F&B context

Inventory is measured at the lower of cost or net realisable value (NRV) under IAS 2 — that doesn't change. What changes is how NRV is assessed: a dairy-based beverage ingredient going past its use-by date has an NRV of zero, not the purchase price. FEFO (first-expired-first-out) discipline, regular short-interval stock counts, and a documented wastage policy are what keep the inventory balance from silently overstating in a perishable goods environment.

The cost formula for F&B ingredients is typically FIFO (first-in-first-out) or weighted average — FIFO is more common in perishables because it mirrors the actual physical flow of stock (oldest items used first under good FEFO practice). LIFO is not permitted under IFRS.

Worked Example — Theoretical vs Actual Food Cost

The most important F&B finance control tool is the gap between theoretical food cost (what you should have used based on recipes × sales) and actual food cost (what you actually purchased, adjusted for opening/closing stock). Any persistent gap between the two is evidence of wastage, theft, portion-size drift, or recipe non-compliance.

Q8You see actual food cost running at 31% of revenue but theoretical food cost is 26%. What are the possible explanations, and how do you investigate?

Four main buckets: wastage — ingredients going past use-by date or being discarded due to preparation errors; portioning inconsistency — baristas making drinks heavier or with more ingredients than the recipe card specifies; shrinkage or theft — a real risk in a cash and consumables environment; and pricing errors or recipe card drift — if menu prices haven't been updated to reflect ingredient cost increases, the theoretical food cost calculation itself becomes stale. Investigate by pulling a physical stock count and comparing to the perpetual record, then cross-referencing the recipe card costing for the top five selling SKUs against actual purchase invoices for those specific ingredients. If the gap concentrates on one or two items rather than spreading across all ingredients proportionally, it usually points to portioning or theft rather than a systematic recipe cost issue.

Q9How often would you expect physical stock counts in a café environment, and why is that different from the FMCG warehouse cadence at Massad or Raad?

Weekly, at minimum — and for the highest-value, shortest-shelf-life items like dairy and specialty coffee beans, potentially daily. That's a fundamentally different rhythm from Massad or Raad, where a monthly cycle count plus a quarterly full physical count is a perfectly reasonable control cadence for goods that don't expire within days. In a café, a four-week gap between counts is long enough for a food cost variance to compound to a material number before anyone catches it. The stock count in F&B is an operational control first and an accounting control second — the operations team should own the weekly count, and finance validates it, rather than finance initiating it entirely.

LC1

What a Letter of Credit Is — and Why It Exists

Plain English

Massad wants to buy EUR 500,000 of Kerrygold butter from a supplier in Ireland. The Irish supplier doesn't know Massad. They're not going to ship half a million euros of butter to Kuwait on a promise that payment will come later. And Massad doesn't want to wire the full amount before the goods leave Cork either. A Letter of Credit is how both sides get what they need: the supplier gets a guarantee from a serious bank that they'll be paid once they can prove they shipped the goods correctly. Massad doesn't pay until that proof exists.

The core logic — payment against documents, not trust

An LC is a conditional payment commitment issued by a bank (the issuing bank, typically in Kuwait) on behalf of a buyer (Massad), promising to pay a seller (the Irish supplier) a specified amount, provided the seller presents a defined set of documents within a defined time frame that prove the goods were shipped as agreed. The bank doesn't care whether the goods are good. It cares only whether the documents match the LC terms precisely — a date one day out of range, a quantity number with a typo, an incorrect port of loading — any discrepancy is grounds to refuse payment. This makes the documentation discipline as commercially important as the commercial deal itself.

Governed by the Uniform Customs and Practice for Documentary Credits — UCP 600 (ICC, 2007). Universally applied by banks handling international trade.

LC Types — Know the Right One Before the Bank Asks

Sight LC

  • Payment made immediately when complying documents presented
  • Cash leaves Massad's account on document presentation
  • Lower bank fee — no deferred payment risk to bank
  • Used for: standard commodity imports where supplier demands immediate payment

Usance (Deferred) LC

  • Payment at a fixed number of days after document presentation (e.g. 60 or 90 days)
  • Massad receives the goods but pays later — effectively a credit period
  • Higher bank fee — bank carries payment risk during deferred period

Revolving LC

  • Automatically reinstates after each drawing, up to a total limit
  • Efficient for repeat orders from the same supplier (e.g. monthly Kerrygold shipments)
  • Eliminates the cost of issuing a fresh LC each time
  • Limit set once; bank monitors cumulative exposure

Transferable LC

  • The original beneficiary (supplier) can transfer rights to a secondary supplier
  • Used when Massad buys through a trading intermediary rather than the manufacturer direct
  • Adds complexity — bank charges for each transfer
  • Relevant for Raad's licence-number-one multi-country sourcing model

Q1At what point does Massad actually own the imported Kerrygold — when it pays the bank, or earlier?

At shipment — when the supplier loads the goods and transfers the Bill of Lading. Under typical international trade terms (CIF or FOB), title and risk pass at the agreed point of shipment, not when payment is made. This is why goods-in-transit accounting exists: from the moment the ship leaves Cork with Massad's Kerrygold on board, those goods are Massad's inventory sitting in transit, and they should be on Massad's balance sheet as such, even though Massad hasn't paid yet and the goods haven't cleared Kuwait customs. A company that only records inventory when it physically arrives at the warehouse is quietly understating both its inventory and its payable throughout the entire transit period.

Q2Why are customs duty and freight capitalised into inventory rather than expensed directly?

IAS 2 defines inventory cost as all costs of purchase, costs of conversion, and other costs incurred in bringing inventory to its present location and condition. Customs duty and freight are squarely within "bringing inventory to its present location" — they are costs Massad had to incur to get the Kerrygold from Cork to the Kuwait warehouse, and without them, Massad couldn't have the inventory at all. Expensing them immediately would misstate gross margin — cost of sales would be understated in the period of import and overstated in the period of sale, since the stock would carry an artificially low cost basis. Capitalising them means COGS reflects the true total landed cost of each unit, which is the right basis for margin management and pricing decisions.

Q3What is the LC cash margin deposit and how should it be presented in the financial statements?

The cash margin is a security deposit the Kuwait bank requires Massad to pledge against the LC it's issuing on their behalf — the bank's protection in case Massad can't settle when the documents present. It's Massad's cash but is restricted — it cannot be used for any other purpose while the LC is outstanding. On the balance sheet, it should be presented separately from free cash, typically as a restricted deposit or LC margin deposit within current assets or financial assets, because including it in the general cash and cash equivalents line would overstate Massad's available liquidity. For banking covenant purposes, any covenant calculated off "cash and cash equivalents" needs to be clear whether restricted deposits are included or excluded — that classification is worth confirming explicitly with the bank at the covenant documentation stage.

BG1

What a Bank Guarantee Is — and Why It's Different

Plain English

An LC is about paying for goods. A Bank Guarantee is about standing behind a promise to perform. If Tazweed signs a lease with a Kuwait City mall and the landlord wants assurance that Tazweed will actually pay the rent, the landlord asks for a Bank Guarantee — a bank's promise to pay the landlord a fixed sum if Tazweed defaults on its obligations. The mall landlord doesn't need the money now; they just need to know it will be there if things go wrong.

A BG is a contingent liability — no immediate cash flow

Unlike an LC, which results in a real cash payment when documents are presented, a BG is activated only if the applicant (Tazweed or Massad) defaults on its underlying obligation. Until that happens, the bank has merely committed to pay — and that commitment may never be called. This makes BGs contingent liabilities in the accounting sense: disclose in the notes, but do not recognise on the main balance sheet unless "more likely than not" to be called (which, for a functioning business, is rarely the case).

The bank, however, treats the BG as a real exposure and charges accordingly — and typically requires either a cash margin or a lien over assets as collateral, just as with an LC.

A BG is governed by ISP98 (International Standby Practices) for standby instruments, or URDG 758 (Uniform Rules for Demand Guarantees) for demand guarantees — Kuwait banks follow one or the other depending on the counterparty jurisdiction.

BG Types & Kuwait-Specific Use Cases

Performance Guarantee

  • Bank guarantees that the applicant will perform a contract as agreed
  • Called if the applicant fails to deliver or complete
  • Used: Raad guaranteeing distribution exclusivity obligations to a principal brand owner; contractor guarantees to a client

Advance Payment Guarantee

  • Bank guarantees return of advance payment if goods/services not delivered
  • Supplier provides this to buyer when buyer pays upfront
  • Used: if Massad makes advance payments to a new European supplier for a large order

Rental / Lease Guarantee

  • Bank guarantees the tenant will pay rent and fulfil lease obligations
  • Landlord can call the guarantee if tenant defaults on rent
  • Used: directly relevant to Tazweed — mall landlords in Kuwait commonly require 3–6 months' rent as BG before granting a lease

Bid / Tender Bond

  • Bank guarantees the bidder will accept a contract if awarded it
  • Called if the awarded bidder withdraws
  • Used: any group entity bidding on government or large corporate tenders

Kuwait-specific BG landscape

In Kuwait, Bank Guarantees are standard and pervasive in commercial relationships — more so than in many Western markets — for several structural reasons: limited public credit registries make credit-checking counterparties difficult, so BGs substitute for that verification; government and semi-government contracts almost universally require bid bonds and performance guarantees; and the real estate and mall sector routinely requires rental guarantees as a condition of tenancy, sometimes replacing traditional security deposits entirely. For Tazweed's store rollout — both Kuwait and Saudi — rental BGs from the group's banking relationships are almost certainly a live requirement for every new store lease signed.

Q4A group-level bank covenant requires total debt not to exceed three times EBITDA. Should outstanding Bank Guarantees be included in "total debt" for this calculation?

It depends entirely on how the covenant documents define "debt" or "financial indebtedness" — and this is not a detail to assume away. Many modern facilities agreements specifically include contingent liabilities such as guarantees and indemnities within the definition of indebtedness, particularly if they are material in aggregate. If the definition is silent or ambiguous, the conservative, defensible position is to include them and request a specific exclusion or carve-out from the bank if they genuinely represent low-risk, never-to-be-called operational guarantees. The worst outcome is to discover mid-year that a literal reading of the covenant definition includes the full BG book, pushing the group into technical default on a ratio it thought it was comfortably inside.

Q5Tazweed is about to open five new stores in Saudi Arabia. Each mall requires a rental BG. What does this mean for the group's banking facility utilisation?

Each BG draws on the group's non-funded guarantee facility limits with its banks — this is separate from cash borrowing but still comes out of the group's overall credit lines. Five new stores, each requiring three to six months' rent as a rental BG, could easily consume KWD 40,000 to 80,000 of guarantee line capacity before a single cup of coffee is sold. That needs to be factored into the banking plan alongside the funded facility needs for working capital and store capex, not treated as a separate, minor treasury issue. I'd want a complete forward view of BG requirements for the entire planned expansion schedule before the first Saudi lease is signed, to confirm the group's existing guarantee capacity is sufficient or whether a facility increase needs to be negotiated.

Q6Mundra asks you to summarise the group's total trade finance and contingent liability position across all three companies. Walk me through exactly what you'd pull together.

Q7A Kuwait supplier tells Massad they'll give better pricing if Massad pays by open account instead of LC. Should Massad agree?

Potentially — but it's a risk-versus-cost trade-off, not a straightforward yes. An LC costs Massad bank commission, ties up cash margin, and adds documentation burden. Open account — paying after goods are received without bank intermediation — is cheaper and simpler. For a known, long-standing domestic supplier in Kuwait with a clean track record, open account terms are perfectly reasonable and many FMCG businesses run most local supplier relationships this way. The LC cost is genuinely unnecessary if the counterparty risk is well understood. Where I'd keep the LC is for new suppliers, large one-off orders with significant advance commitment, or any cross-border supplier where Massad has limited practical recourse if delivery fails — because the LC's document-control mechanism is the only tool that makes payment genuinely conditional on proof of compliant shipment.
