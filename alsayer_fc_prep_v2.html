Franchise Fee and Royalty Accounting

IFRS 15 / IAS 38
Plain English First

Al Sayer pays Caribou and Five Guys two types of fees: a large upfront lump sum to acquire territorial rights, and monthly royalties based on sales. These are treated very differently — the upfront fee becomes a balance sheet asset; the royalties are a monthly operating expense.

Fee typeAccounting treatmentStandard

Ongoing royalty (~5–8% of net sales)Expense in the period as incurred. Accrue monthly based on confirmed net sales. USD-denominated — retranslate at closing rate each period. FX gain/loss in P&L.IAS 37 / IAS 21
Marketing fund (~1–2% of net sales)Expense as incurred unless Al Sayer acts as agent for a pass-through fund (net presentation). Most likely expensed directly.IFRS 15
Sub-franchise income (if applicable)Recognise over time as performance obligation (brand licence + ongoing support) is satisfied. Variable royalties recognised as sub-franchisee generates sales.IFRS 15

2.2

IFRS 16 — Lease Accounting (~300 stores, all leased)

IFRS 16
Plain English First

Every store location is rented. Before IFRS 16, rent was just an operating expense. Now, every lease above 12 months creates a Right-of-Use (ROU) asset on the balance sheet and a matching lease liability. With ~300 stores, this is one of the largest balance sheet items in the company — and the FC owns every line of it.

ElementWhat the FC must know
Recognition triggerLease term >12 months AND not low-value → recognise ROU Asset + Lease Liability at commencement
MeasurementPresent value of future lease payments, discounted at Incremental Borrowing Rate (IBR) — the rate Al Sayer could borrow at for similar term and collateral in Kuwait
ROU AssetClassified under PPE. Depreciated straight-line over the shorter of lease term or useful life. Depreciation replaces rent in the P&L — it does NOT equal the old rent figure.
Lease LiabilityUnwound using effective interest method. Each payment = interest component (finance cost in P&L) + principal repayment (reduces the liability on BS)
Variable rent (% of sales)Exclude from lease liability. Expense in the period as sales occur. Very common in mall leases in Kuwait — must be tracked separately from the fixed element.
Renewal optionsInclude in lease term only if reasonably certain to exercise. Management judgement required — audit flashpoint. Document rationale per store.
Lease modificationsStore closures, rent concessions, extensions — each triggers remeasurement of both ROU Asset and Lease Liability. High-frequency event at 300 stores.
P&L impact vs old IAS 17EBITDA improves (depreciation and interest are below EBITDA; old rent was above). Net profit impact = higher charge in early years of lease (reverses later). Leverage ratios increase (liabilities on BS).
FC's master lease schedule must containPer store: commencement date · lease term · IBR · annual payment schedule · ROU opening balance · annual depreciation · lease liability opening balance · annual interest · annual principal repayment · ROU closing NBV · Lease liability closing balance
2.3

IAS 29 — Hyperinflationary Accounting (Turkey)

Advanced
Plain English First

Turkey has experienced very high inflation. When a country reaches cumulative inflation above 100% over 3 years, IFRS requires the subsidiary's financials to be restated in today's purchasing power before translating to KWD. This is technically one of the most complex areas in IFRS and one of the highest-impact areas for Al Sayer that most interview candidates won't prepare for.

StepWhat happens
1. Determine applicabilityCumulative 3-year inflation >100% → IAS 29 applies. Turkey has met this threshold. FC monitors the IASB hyperinflationary economies list annually.
2. Restate non-monetary itemsApply Turkish consumer price index (CPI) factor to PPE, inventory, intangibles, equity — express in current purchasing power. Monetary items (cash, receivables, payables) not restated — already in current units.
3. Gain/loss on net monetary positionHolding net monetary liabilities during inflation = gain (purchasing power of debt erodes). Holding net monetary assets = loss. Recognised in P&L.
4. Translate to KWD (IAS 21)After IAS 29 restatement, translate at closing rate (not average rate — IAS 21 uses closing rate for hyperinflationary subsidiaries). Translation differences → OCI.
FC practical roleYou likely won't perform the IAS 29 restatement yourself — use specialist support. But you must review, challenge, and present the output to auditors and to the CFO/CEO. Demonstrate you understand the mechanics.

Store-Level P&L — 4-Wall EBITDA

Why "4-Wall"?

4-wall EBITDA = what happens within the four walls of a single store, before any central overhead allocation. It is the primary KPI for evaluating whether an individual store is financially justified. FC must produce this for every store, every month.

POS-to-GL Reconciliation

Plain English First

Every store's POS records every sale in real time. The FC must ensure what the POS records matches exactly what gets booked in the accounting system. With 300 stores, this is a massive daily discipline — any unexplained gap means either revenue is leaking or the system is broken.

Q 03CFOWhat does a Financial Controller actually do differently from a Finance Manager?

A Finance Manager executes within a defined process. An FC owns the process — and the numbers before they leave finance. The distinction is accountability: if there's a reconciliation gap, an IFRS misapplication, or a control failure, it stops at the FC's desk.

Practically in F&B: the FC designs the chart of accounts, sets the food cost reporting cadence, owns the IFRS 16 lease schedule, reviews monthly payroll accruals, approves journal entries before close, and challenges store managers when 4-wall EBITDA deteriorates. The Finance Manager might produce the report. The FC determines whether the report is telling the truth.

Anchor line: "The FC owns the numbers before they leave finance. The buck stops here."
3.2

Technical Questions

Q 04CFOHow would you set up the month-end close for a 300-store operation?

I'd run a phased close on a 4-day cycle:

  • Day 1: All store POS data in GL. Cash and card reconciliations signed off by store managers. Gaps flagged same day — not carried to Day 2.
  • Day 2: COGS journals posted from actual month-end stock counts. Payroll accruals posted (actual hours). IFRS 16 amortisation and interest entries posted from the master lease schedule.
  • Day 3: Royalty accruals on confirmed net sales. Prepayment and accrual schedule reviewed. Intercompany reconciliations if applicable. Bank reconciliation completed.
  • Day 4: Final review. P&L and BS flash report to CFO/CEO. Variance commentary: actual vs budget by brand, by store tier, by region.

This only works if the infrastructure is in place first: a maintained IFRS 16 lease schedule, a food cost theoretical model per brand, a payroll accrual calculator, and a close checklist the team follows without me chasing them.

Anchor line: "4-day close, systemised. Infrastructure first — speed comes from having the right foundations in place."
Q 05CFOWe have a store where food cost keeps running at 38%, vs our theoretical 30%. Walk me through how you'd investigate.

8% variance on food cost is significant. At 30% of revenue, that's nearly a 27% cost overrun on the COGS line. I'd investigate in five layers:

  • Layer 1 — Data integrity: Is the 38% calculated correctly? Are all sales hitting the POS? Is the stock count accurate? Sometimes the issue is a recording error, not actual over-consumption.
  • Layer 2 — Supplier pricing: Have ingredient costs increased? Check supplier invoices vs recipe cost assumptions. Price rises without menu price adjustments compress margin immediately — often masked as a "food cost problem."
  • Layer 3 — Wastage: Pull the wastage log for this store. Is it being recorded? Unrecorded wastage is the most common source of unexplained food cost variance — particularly at Five Guys with fresh beef.
  • Layer 4 — Portioning: Check actual portion weights vs recipe specifications. Staff giving larger portions than the recipe specifies. Common in stores with low supervision, especially with customisable items like Five Guys.
  • Layer 5 — Theft or fraud: If layers 1–4 show nothing, I'm looking at unauthorised consumption, gifts to customers, or inventory leaving the store.

I want 3–6 months of data to determine whether this is structural or event-driven before making any accusations.

Anchor line: "Start with data integrity before assuming operational failure. 5-layer investigation, systematic. Don't accuse the GM before you've checked the recording."
Q 06CFOExplain how IFRS 16 affects our P&L and balance sheet compared to the old lease standard.

Under old IAS 17, operating leases were entirely off-balance-sheet. Rent was a single straight-line expense in P&L — simple, but it understated leverage for businesses with large lease commitments.

Under IFRS 16: every lease above 12 months creates a Right-of-Use asset (asset side of BS) and a Lease Liability (liability side). Both measured at present value of future lease payments discounted at the incremental borrowing rate at commencement.

P&L impact: The old rent expense disappears. Replaced by: (1) depreciation of the ROU asset — classified as an operating expense, straight-line, and (2) interest on the lease liability — finance cost, unwinding using effective interest method. In early years, combined charge exceeds old rent; decreases over time as liability reduces.

EBITDA impact: EBITDA improves under IFRS 16 — both depreciation and interest are below the EBITDA line. The old rent was above EBITDA. For a 300-store operator, this is a significant reported EBITDA improvement on paper.

Balance sheet impact: Debt ratios increase because lease liabilities now appear on the balance sheet. Any loan covenants tied to leverage ratios need to be assessed against this.

For Al Sayer with ~300 leases, the FC must maintain a complete lease schedule — updated for every store commencement, modification, extension, and closure. This is the FC's most complex continuous accounting task.

Anchor line: "IFRS 16 brought leases onto the balance sheet — EBITDA improves, leverage ratios increase. 300 leases means this is one of the largest balance sheet items we have."
Q 07CFOTurkey has high inflation. How does that affect how we report those operations?

Turkey triggers IAS 29 — hyperinflationary accounting — when cumulative 3-year inflation exceeds 100%. Turkey has met this threshold and remains on the IASB's hyperinflationary economies list.

Practically: before translating the Turkey subsidiary's financials into KWD, the statements must first be restated in today's purchasing power. Non-monetary items — PPE, inventory, intangibles — are multiplied by a price index factor. Monetary items (cash, receivables, payables) are not restated — they're already in current nominal units.

The net effect on P&L of the monetary position during hyperinflation: holding net monetary liabilities = gain (purchasing power of debt erodes). Holding net monetary assets = loss. This hits the consolidated P&L.

After IAS 29 restatement, translate to KWD at the closing exchange rate — IAS 21 uses closing rate for hyperinflationary subsidiaries, not the average rate that would normally apply. Translation differences go to OCI.

In practice: specialist support for Turkey restatement calculations — technically complex, high audit sensitivity. But as FC, I review, challenge, and own the output.

Anchor line: "IAS 29 restatement before IAS 21 translation. Closing rate applies. Specialist support for the calculation — FC owns the review."

Q 09CEOA store GM tells you food cost variance is supplier price increases. Your data says it's wastage. How do you handle that?

I lead with the data, not the accusation. I sit with the GM and show the wastage log, the supplier invoice history, and the theoretical vs actual comparison side by side. If supplier prices are flat and wastage is the culprit, I let the numbers do the talking.

I also give the GM an out: maybe the wastage recording system is broken, not the actual behaviour. That's a process failure, not a character failure — and it's fixable. I offer to help fix it.

What I don't do: accept an explanation that contradicts the data without evidence. Finance's role is to maintain integrity of the numbers — not to be popular with GMs. But I do it respectfully, because I need GMs to see me as a partner, not an auditor out to catch them.

Anchor line: "Lead with data, give them an out, stay firm. Finance must maintain integrity — but relationships matter for the long term."

COGS calculationOpening stock + purchases – closing stock = COGS consumed in the periodFC must ensure stock count accuracy — any error in closing stock flows directly and entirely into COGS and gross profit.

IAS 21 — Foreign Currency Effects

FX Exposure
Three FX exposures at Al Sayer

1. Royalty payments to US franchisors (USD-denominated). 2. Turkey subsidiary (TRY → KWD). 3. Any other foreign currency transactions (supplier invoices, FF&E imports, etc.).

Transaction typeIAS 21 treatment
Royalty payable to US franchisor (USD)Recognise at spot rate on accrual date. Retranslate monetary liability at closing rate each period. Exchange difference (gain or loss) recognised in P&L.
Turkey subsidiary (TRY → KWD)If IAS 29 applies: restate to current purchasing power first (see Vol 2), then translate at closing rate. Translation differences in OCI (not P&L).
Normal foreign subsidiary (if any)Assets and liabilities at closing rate. Income and expenses at average rate (or transaction rate). Translation difference → OCI (foreign currency translation reserve in equity).
FX risk managementNatural hedge possible: if Turkey operations generate USD revenue, use to offset USD royalty payables. If formal hedging instruments used → IFRS 9 hedge accounting criteria must be met and documented.
4.4

IAS 36 — Impairment of Assets

Annual Review
Plain English First

If a store is consistently losing money, or the master franchise right is carried at more than its recoverable value, the assets must be written down. IAS 36 controls the process. For a 300-store operator, this is an annual exercise — and can affect reported profit materially.

TriggerIAS 36 response
Store 4-wall EBITDA persistently negativeImpairment indicator. Test the store's asset group (ROU asset + fit-out + equipment) against its recoverable amount = higher of Fair Value Less Costs to Sell (FVLCTS) and Value in Use (VIU from DCF of expected future cash flows).
Master franchise intangibleIndefinite useful life → annual impairment test required regardless of indicators. Finite useful life → amortise + impairment test only when indicators present.
Store closure decisionAssets written off. ROU asset and lease liability derecognised. Exit costs (lease termination penalties, fit-out removal) provisioned under IAS 37. Any impairment loss in P&L.
Turkey operations (country-level indicator)Significant adverse economic change (TRY devaluation, hyperinflation) = impairment indicator for Turkey-based assets. Test all Turkey CGUs.

5.2

Kuwait 5% Tax Retention — Law No. 2 of 2008

Applied Directly to Royalties
Specific to Al Sayer: Ongoing royalty payments to Caribou Coffee (US) and Five Guys (US) are payments to foreign contractors. Both trigger the 5% retention obligation under Kuwait Law No. 2 of 2008. This is one of the FC's most important compliance obligations — non-compliance makes Al Sayer directly liable to the Kuwait Tax Authority (KTA) for the full retention amount.
ElementDetail
Legal obligationAl Sayer must withhold 5% from all payments to foreign (non-Kuwaiti) contractors and service providers, including US franchisors, until they provide a Tax Clearance Certificate (TCC) or No Objection Letter (NOL) from the KTA.
Scope for Al SayerMonthly royalty to Caribou Coffee (US) · Monthly royalty to Five Guys (US) · Any other foreign contractor payments (consulting fees, equipment maintenance from foreign vendors, etc.)
ProcessUS franchisor registers the contract with KTA → obtains TCC or NOL → provides to Al Sayer. Until TCC/NOL received, Al Sayer releases only 95% of each payment and holds 5% in a tax retention payable account.
FC accounting entryDr Royalty Payable [full amount] → Cr Cash [95%] + Cr Tax Retention Payable — KTA [5%]
Failure consequencesAl Sayer becomes directly liable to KTA for the 5% on every non-withheld payment. Given volume and USD size of royalty payments, cumulative exposure can be substantial.
FC tracking requirementMaintain a register of TCC/NOL status per foreign contractor, per contract year. Flag expiry dates — certificates must be renewed. Never release 100% without valid TCC/NOL on file.
