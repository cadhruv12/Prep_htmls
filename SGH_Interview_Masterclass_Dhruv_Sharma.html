Zakat — Complete Framework for SGH as GFD

What it is: Mandatory Islamic wealth tax — 2.5% of zakatable wealth. NOT an income tax. Calculated on asset base, not profit. A loss-making year can still generate Zakat if zakatable assets are significant.

Zakatable assets: Cash and equivalents, trade receivables (net of ECL provisions), development properties held for sale (inventory), short-term Murabaha placements, Wakala deposits.

NOT zakatable: Investment property (IAS 40 — long-term rental asset), PP&E, goodwill, long-term strategic equity investments.

Deductions: Current liabilities due within the year — trade payables, accrued expenses, short-term Murabaha payables.

Formula: (Zakatable assets − Current liabilities due within year) × 2.5% = Zakat payable

Process as GFD: Prepare Zakat workpaper from consolidated balance sheet → categorise every asset/liability → apply formula → present to SSB for certification → include in year-end board pack as part of profit appropriation (alongside dividend and statutory reserve) → disclose as a separate P&L line after PBT.

Statutory Reserve: Separate obligation — 10% of annual PAT must be transferred to a statutory reserve until it reaches 50% of paid-up capital. Non-distributable. Mandatory for all K.S.C.C companies under Kuwait Companies Law.

Core Sharia Principles

Islamic Finance

Four principles underpin every Islamic finance instrument. Understand these and the instruments are logical extensions. Misunderstand them and everything looks like a workaround.

The One Sentence That Explains Everything

In Islamic finance, money is not a commodity that can be rented for a price. Money is only a medium of exchange. To earn a return, you must take real economic risk — by owning an asset, sharing profit and loss, or providing a genuine service.

Principle 1 — Prohibition of Riba (Interest) · ربا

Riba means "excess." Any predetermined fixed return on money lent — regardless of business outcome — is Riba and is forbidden. The key word is predetermined. If the return is fixed before the economic outcome is known, it is Riba.

Your current world (Bustan/ANDS): Gulf Bank lends KD 5M at 5.5% p.a. Monthly interest = outstanding balance × 5.5% ÷ 12. The bank rents money and earns a guaranteed return. That is Riba.

SGH world: KFH buys the asset SGH needs for KD 5M, sells it to SGH for KD 5.75M payable over 3 years. The KD 750K is a trade profit on a real sale — fixed at inception, never changing. The bank owned the asset and took (brief) ownership risk. Not Riba.

The Murabaha markup is NOT Riba — this is the most important distinction to know. Riba is a return on money. Murabaha profit is a return on a trade. Confusing them is the most common mistake.

Principle 2 — Profit & Loss Sharing (PLS)

To earn a return you must share economic risk. Financer and entrepreneur share actual profits — and losses. This is the spirit of Mudarabah and Musharakah. No guaranteed return either way.

Your analogy: Every capital allocation decision you made at Bustan Group was Mudarabah in economic substance. You (holding company) were the Rab-ul-Maal. The subsidiary GM was the Mudarib. Capital deployed, expertise applied, profits flow back to the holding company. Same economic logic.

Principle 3 — Asset-Backing Requirement

Every transaction must be tied to a real, tangible asset or genuine service. You cannot trade money for money at a markup. The asset must exist first. This is why Islamic banks were far less exposed in 2008 — they could not create synthetic derivatives with no underlying assets.

SGH angle: SGH's 2,000+ units and 5 malls ARE the underlying real assets behind every Sukuk issued, every Murabaha structured, every Ijara charged. The business model is inherently asset-backed at its core.

Principle 4 — Prohibition of Gharar & Maysir · غرر / ميسر

Gharar = excessive contractual uncertainty about key terms. Maysir = gambling/speculation. Both forbidden. This prohibits speculative derivatives, conventional insurance (replaced by Takaful), and zero-sum contracts.

Important nuance: Normal business risk is NOT Gharar. A Musharakah JV may lose money — that is acceptable. Gharar specifically targets uncertainty about the key contract terms: what is being exchanged, at what price, under what conditions. Construction contracts with clear scope, price, and timeline are fine. Open-ended vague contracts are not.

Islamic Finance Instruments — Deep Dive with Numbers

Islamic Finance

Eight instruments. Each explained with your career as the reference point, SGH as the application, and real numbers where they matter most.

1. Murabaha — Cost-Plus Sale · مرابحة

Bank buys an asset the client needs, sells it at a disclosed markup payable over time. Total profit is fixed at inception — never a running percentage on an outstanding balance. This distinction is everything.

Early settlement trap: In a conventional loan, paying early saves future interest. In Murabaha, the total sale price (e.g. KD 11.4M) is legally owed because it was agreed as a sale price — unless the contract has an Ibra (rebate) clause. Always check your Murabaha contracts for Ibra before planning early settlement.

Al-Riyada context: Al-Riyada buys assets for clients and sells at a markup — that spread is Al-Riyada's primary revenue. Recognised using EIR method under IFRS 9 over the facility life, not upfront.

2. Ijara — Islamic Lease · إجارة

Bank/owner retains ownership of an asset and charges rent for its use. Two variants: pure operating lease (no ownership transfer) and Ijara wa Iqtina (lease-to-own — nominal transfer at maturity).

SGH's core business IS Ijara: Every tenant in SGH's 2,000+ units and 5 malls is in an Ijara arrangement. SGH owns the property (IAS 40), charges rent (100% Halal — rent for use of an owned asset), recognises income straight-line over lease term. SGH's entire primary revenue model is Ijara.

Diminishing Musharakah: KFH and SGH co-own a KD 8M property (KFH 60%, SGH 40%). SGH pays rent on KFH's share AND monthly instalments buying down KFH's share. As KFH's share reduces from 60% to 35%, rent reduces proportionally — charged only on KFH's remaining portion. At the end SGH owns 100%. This is Kuwait's Islamic mortgage, used by KFH and Boubyan Bank.

3. Sukuk — Asset-Backed Certificates · صكوك

Not bonds. A bond = debt (lend money, receive interest). Sukuk = ownership certificate (own a share of a real asset, receive its income).

Sharia form vs IFRS substance: Under Sharia, Sukuk holders are co-owners (equity-like). Under IFRS IAS 32, if SGH has a contractual obligation to make distributions and redeem at face value at maturity, it is a financial liability — regardless of the co-ownership form. On SGH's consolidated balance sheet: Sukuk = liability. Distributions = Islamic financing charges in P&L, not dividends.

Sukuk income is NOT dividend income: It is Islamic finance income (EIR-based for amortised cost classification). Dividends come from equity instruments. Sukuk investors under IFRS hold debt-like instruments — income is finance income, not equity income. The one exception: Perpetual Sukuk with no redemption obligation — used by Islamic banks for Tier 1 capital — qualifies as equity under IAS 32. Not relevant for SGH's capital structure.

4. Mudarabah — Silent Partnership · مضاربة

Rab-ul-Maal provides capital. Mudarib provides expertise and management. Profits split at agreed ratio. Losses fall ONLY on capital provider — unless the Mudarib was negligent. Negligence voids the protection entirely and shifts the full loss to the negligent Mudarib.

Al-Riyada Finance as Rab-ul-Maal deploys KD 2M into a real estate Mudarabah. Developer manages the project. 30% return → split per agreed ratio. Project fails → Al-Riyada loses capital, developer loses time and effort only (unless negligent).

5. Musharakah — Joint Venture · مشاركة

Both parties contribute capital AND management. Both share profits AND losses proportionally. Distinguished from Mudarabah by one rule: BOTH parties bear financial losses. This is a genuine equity joint venture.

SGH GCC expansion: SGH contributes KD 10M cash to a Saudi JV, partner contributes land and licences. Both manage. Both share profits 55/45. Both share losses proportionally. This is Musharakah — neither party has a guaranteed return.

6. Wakala — Agency Agreement · وكالة

Principal appoints agent to invest funds for a fee. Agent targets a specific return — any surplus is the agent's incentive. Return is not guaranteed. If the bank achieves 3.9% against a 4.2% target, SGH receives 3.9%. The target is a benchmark, not a promise. This is the critical operational difference from a conventional fixed deposit.

7. Takaful — Islamic Insurance · تكافل

Conventional insurance = Gharar (uncertain payout) + Maysir (speculative). Takaful = mutual contribution pool. Participants donate to a shared fund. Claims paid from the pool. Surplus returned to participants at year end. No one profits from others' misfortune.

With 5 malls and 2,000+ units, property Takaful premiums are a material SGH cost line. As GFD, ensuring all property and casualty cover is via Takaful providers (Gulf Takaful, Warba Insurance Kuwait) — not conventional insurers — is part of your annual Sharia compliance review. Zero-interest conventional bank accounts are also insufficient — SGH must bank with licensed Islamic banks.

HR Round — Questions & Coached Answers

Round 1 · Noura Jlassi

The HR round decides three things: are you who your CV says you are, are you a cultural and values fit for a Sharia-compliant Kuwaiti holding company, and are you genuinely motivated for this specific role. Treat Noura Jlassi as an intelligent business professional — not a gatekeeper.

Four Golden Rules for This Round

1. Person first, professional second — connect genuinely, not performatively. 2. Specific always beats generic — "At Bustan Group with 13 P&Ls..." not "I have strong leadership skills." 3. Confidence + humility — walk in as a peer, not a supplicant. 4. Every answer lands on SGH — close each response with a connection to this specific role.

HR·01OpenerTell me about yourself.
The first 90 seconds sets the entire tone. HR judges communication clarity, confidence, and whether your story is coherent. This is your controlled narrative — don't waste it.

Your Answer

"I am a Chartered Accountant with 20 years of finance leadership experience — the last decade entirely in Kuwait and the GCC, across FMCG, distribution, and multi-entity holding structures. Currently I serve as Group Finance Director at a Kuwaiti holding company where I oversee 13 P&Ls across 7 companies, lead an 18-person finance team, and am responsible for everything from IFRS consolidation and board reporting to treasury, ERP implementation, and capital allocation. My career has been built on one consistent thread: taking complex, multi-entity environments and building finance functions that give leadership the clarity they need to make better decisions faster. I am drawn to SGH specifically because the combination of real estate, finance, and Sharia-compliant operations represents the kind of multi-dimensional finance leadership challenge I want to grow into at GFD level."

Coach NotePractise until it is fluent, not recited. 90 seconds maximum. End on SGH — not on yourself.
HR·02MotivationWhy are you leaving your current role? Why now?
Three hidden fears: were you pushed out, is this purely about salary, will you leave SGH in 14 months too? Your Bustan tenure is only ~14 months — the interviewer will notice. Address it proactively, not defensively.

Standard Answer

"My reason is genuinely forward-looking. At my current group I was brought in to build the finance infrastructure — SAP implementation, consolidation framework, board reporting standardisation. That build phase is substantially complete. The role has shifted from building to maintaining — and I am honest enough to know that maintenance is not where I do my best work. I am a builder. SGH represents the opposite situation — a finance function with significant room to evolve alongside an active GCC expansion agenda and a Sharia-compliant framework I find genuinely compelling. I have no negative relationship with my current employer."

If Challenged Directly on 14 Months

"Fair observation — 14 months is shorter than my usual tenure and I want to address it directly. The role was positioned as a transformation mandate during recruitment. In practice, the restructuring context meant it became a stabilisation role — legitimate work, but not what I do best. I delivered what was asked. I am not leaving a problem. I am moving toward a better fit. SGH is where I want to do the most meaningful work of the next 10 years."

Never say: "Better growth opportunities" (meaningless). "My boss and I have different styles" (signals conflict). "The salary wasn't keeping pace" (save for offer stage).
Always include: "I have no negative relationship with my current employer." Neutralises the hidden fear before they voice it.

IFRS Standards — Mapped to SGH Operations

Technical · 8 Key Standards

The standards you will live by as GFD. Each one mapped to SGH's specific business with the key judgement calls and your talking points highlighted.

IFRS·16LeasesLeases — both sides: SGH as lessor (2,000+ units, 5 malls) and as lessee (Ijara financing, office leases)

SGH as Lessor — Core Business

All 2,000+ units and 5 malls are operating leases from SGH's perspective. Rental income = straight-line over each lease term regardless of payment timing. Advance rents received = deferred income (current liability) until earned. Investment property stays on balance sheet at IAS 40 Fair Value — not derecognised. Vacancy incentives (rent-free periods) = reduce the recognised straight-line income over the full lease term.

SGH as Lessee — Ijara Financing / Office Leases

Any Ijara wa Iqtina (lease-to-own) financing SGH takes: IFRS 16 finance lease treatment. Recognise (1) ROU asset — PV of future Ijara rentals, depreciated over shorter of lease term or useful life, and (2) Ijara liability — unwound at the rate implicit in the Ijara or SGH's incremental Islamic borrowing rate. Monthly P&L: depreciation charge + Ijara finance charge. The discount rate used must be documented and applied consistently.

Key Judgement Call

Determining the appropriate discount rate for Ijara liabilities where the implicit rate is not provided by the bank. Use SGH's incremental Murabaha profit rate — the rate SGH would pay for equivalent Sharia financing at that date. This must be periodically reviewed and updated when new Ijara arrangements are entered.

IAS·40Inv. PropertyInvestment Property — SGH's most material standard. KD 120M+ of malls and units classified here.

Classification

Property held to earn rental income or for capital appreciation (not own use, not for sale in ordinary course) = Investment Property. SGH's malls and rental units qualify. Annual accounting policy choice: Cost Model (carry at cost less depreciation) or Fair Value Model (revalue annually, all changes in P&L). Most GCC property groups use Fair Value. The policy must be applied to the entire Investment Property class consistently.

Fair Value Model — The Critical P&L Impact

Unlike IAS 16 revaluation (gains to OCI), under IAS 40 Fair Value Model ALL changes go directly to P&L. A 5% uplift on a KD 100M portfolio = KD 5M gain in reported profit — entirely non-cash. Implication: always present "underlying profit excluding fair value movements" alongside reported profit in board packs. Dividend policy must be based on cash profit, not fair-value-inflated PAT. Distributing a non-cash IAS 40 gain as dividend drains real cash from the business.

Valuation Process

Independent RICS-qualified valuers annually. Methodology — income capitalisation or DCF of rental income — must be disclosed in notes. Auditors will scrutinise: cap rates, vacancy assumptions, rental growth forecasts, comparable transactions. Valuer must be independent of SGH.

IAS 40 vs IAS 36 Interaction

Under Fair Value Model — IAS 36 impairment testing does NOT apply to investment properties. The annual fair value already reflects any decline. IAS 36 still applies to goodwill (annual CGU test), cost-model assets, and Rawsam Engineering's PP&E.

IFRS·9Fin. InstrumentsFinancial Instruments — Murabaha receivables, tenant ECL, Sukuk classification, IPRS hedge accounting

Murabaha Receivables at Al-Riyada (Financial Assets)

Business model: hold to collect. SPPI test: typically passes for vanilla Murabaha — cash flows are solely payments of principal and profit. Classification: amortised cost. Income: EIR method over facility life — not upfront. ECL: 3-stage model. Stage 1 (performing, 12-month ECL). Stage 2 (significant credit deterioration, lifetime ECL). Stage 3 (credit-impaired/default, lifetime ECL — income recognised on net carrying amount only, not gross).

Tenant Receivables ECL

Simplified approach permitted for trade receivables. Build a provision matrix by aging bucket using historical loss rates adjusted for forward-looking macro factors (Kuwait real estate vacancy rates, tenant sector health). With 2,000+ tenants this matrix is material and audit-sensitive.

IPRS Hedge Accounting

Three conditions for cash flow hedge: (1) Formal designation and documentation at inception — no backdating, ever. (2) Economic relationship between IPRS and Murabaha facility. (3) Hedge effectiveness within 80–125% range. If all three met: effective portion to OCI (Cash Flow Hedge Reserve). Recycled to P&L when Murabaha charge hits P&L — net result is flat finance cost line. Miss documentation at inception = FVTPL = P&L volatility. No exceptions.

IFRS·15RevenueRevenue — applies to Rawsam Engineering, property development sales, and Al-Riyada fees. NOT to rental income.

Critical First Point — Rental Income is NOT IFRS 15

Rental income is SGH's primary revenue line — but it is completely outside IFRS 15 scope. It is recognised under IAS 40 / IFRS 16 (lessor) on a straight-line basis over the lease term. Confusing this is the most common IFRS 15 mistake in real estate contexts. Know it cold.

Rawsam Engineering — Over Time Recognition

Long-term construction contracts: recognise over time if control transfers progressively. Input method (costs incurred ÷ total estimated costs) or output method (physical progress). Policy choice — applied consistently. Progress certificates from project managers drive the monthly accrual. Retentions only recognised when highly probable of receipt.

Development Sales — The Off-Plan Question

Off-plan sales: over-time recognition only if (a) SGH has enforceable right to payment for work done to date AND (b) asset has no alternative use. If both conditions met → % completion. If not → point-in-time on legal handover. At 0% construction, revenue is likely zero if point-in-time applies — regardless of contracts signed and deposits received.

IFRS·3M&ABusiness Combinations — triggered when SGH acquires a company. Goodwill, PPA, and annual impairment.

Asset vs Business Acquisition

Buying a single property = asset acquisition (no IFRS 3, no goodwill, transaction costs capitalised). Buying a property company = business combination (IFRS 3 applies, goodwill may arise, transaction costs expensed). The classification determines accounting treatment entirely — it is one of the first questions to resolve in any SGH acquisition.

Goodwill

Excess of consideration over net identifiable assets at fair value at acquisition date. NOT amortised under IFRS. Allocated to CGUs and tested for impairment annually under IAS 36. If CGU's recoverable amount falls below carrying amount (including allocated goodwill) → impairment charge to P&L. This is why overpaying at acquisition has a long financial tail.

For SGH's GCC Expansion

Any corporate acquisition in UAE or Saudi triggers IFRS 3 regardless of jurisdiction. PPA required — identify all intangible assets: brand, customer relationships, below-market leases, development permits, management contracts. Each measured at fair value at acquisition date. Residual = goodwill. The PPA determines amortisation charges for identifiable intangibles going forward.

IFRS·10ConsolidationConsolidated Financials — your core daily task. Includes SPV consolidation for Sukuk structures.

SGH Group Structure

SGH (parent) consolidates: Qurain International RE, Al-Riyada Finance, Rawsam Engineering, Dar Al Shuwaikh, any Sukuk SPVs. Control assessment: power over the entity + exposure to variable returns + ability to use that power. Wholly-owned subsidiaries straightforward. Sukuk SPVs: SGH likely controls them (created for SGH's benefit, SGH holds repurchase obligation) → must consolidate → Sukuk certificates outstanding appear as financial liability in consolidated balance sheet.

Key Monthly Eliminations

Intercompany Murabaha between entities (Al-Riyada lends to Qurain RE): eliminate both the Murabaha receivable at Al-Riyada and the Murabaha payable at Qurain RE, AND eliminate the intercompany "Islamic finance income" in Al-Riyada's P&L and "Islamic financing charge" in Qurain RE's P&L. Management fees from SGH holding to subsidiaries: eliminate holding fee income and subsidiary fee expense. These are the most error-prone eliminations — build a standing intercompany matrix updated monthly.

Your Credential

You currently consolidate 13 P&Ls across 7 companies at Bustan Group. This is IFRS 10 in daily practice. It is your single strongest qualification alignment to SGH's core GFD requirement. Lead with it.

IAS·19Employee BenefitsEmployee Benefits — EOSI is a material obligation in Kuwait for any multi-entity group.

Kuwait EOSI

Kuwait Labour Law: 15 working days per year for first 5 years, 1 month per year thereafter (indefinite contracts). For a group with 50–100+ employees across 4 subsidiaries, this is a material defined benefit obligation. IAS 19 treatment: actuarial valuation for material amounts. Current service cost to P&L. Actuarial gains/losses to OCI. Obligation on balance sheet as non-current liability.

Annual Leave Accrual

Kuwait Labour Law: minimum 30 working days annual leave, accumulates, encashable. Must accrue at current salary rate — not the rate at which leave was earned. Common inconsistency in GCC groups: some entities use historical rates, others use current rates. Standardise from day one.

Your Day 30 Action

Audit EOSI provision calculation methodology across all SGH entities. Inconsistent treatment is one of the most common audit findings in GCC family businesses — some use service years only with no actuarial discount, some with current salary, some with outdated formulas. One standardised approach across all four subsidiaries from the first close cycle you own.

IAS·23Borrowing CostsBorrowing Costs — Murabaha profit during construction of qualifying assets must be capitalised, not expensed.

The Rule

Borrowing costs directly attributable to acquiring, constructing, or producing a qualifying asset must be capitalised as part of that asset's cost. A qualifying asset = one that necessarily takes a substantial period to get ready for use or sale. Real estate developments clearly qualify. Routine inventory that is produced in large quantities on a repetitive basis does not.

Islamic Finance Application

Murabaha profit charges and Ijara rental costs incurred during the construction period of qualifying development properties are capitalised under IAS 23. This means SGH's development property balance sheet carrying amount includes these Islamic financing costs as part of cost. Post-completion → all charges go to P&L. For general borrowings (not property-specific): capitalisation rate = weighted average Islamic financing cost across SGH's borrowing portfolio.

Audit Risk

Capitalising financing costs during construction reduces reported P&L expense in the build phase — inflates profit. Auditors scrutinise this. Must be able to demonstrate the direct link between the specific Murabaha facility and the qualifying asset under construction. Document at facility inception.

T·07IFRS 9How should SGH account for a Murabaha financing facility used to purchase a property?

The profit element (total sale price minus cost) is amortised using the Effective Interest Rate method — mechanically identical to conventional interest expense accounting.
T·08WakalaSGH places KD 5M on Wakala at a target return of 4.2%. The bank achieves only 3.9%. SGH receives:
Explanation: The most operationally important distinction between Wakala and a conventional fixed deposit. In a conventional fixed deposit, the bank guarantees the stated rate regardless of actual investment performance. In Wakala, the return is a share of actual profits achieved. If the bank achieves 3.9%, SGH receives 3.9%. The 4.2% target is a benchmark, not a guarantee. This is what makes it Sharia-compliant — no guaranteed return on money.
T·09IFRS 9SGH enters an IPRS but does NOT formally document the hedging relationship at inception. Six months later the IPRS has a positive fair value of KD 480,000. Where does this appear?
Explanation: Hedge accounting under IFRS 9 is not automatic and cannot be backdated — ever. Without formal designation and documentation at inception, the IPRS is treated as a held-for-trading derivative at FVTPL. The KD 480,000 gain goes directly to P&L, creating exactly the volatility the hedge was supposed to eliminate. Day-one documentation is not a formality — it is the legal trigger for hedge accounting treatment.
T·10TreasuryLME copper prices fall 8% on the day SGH has a KD 3M Commodity Murabaha placement outstanding with KFH. The treasury manager says SGH has lost KD 240,000. Correct?

This does not change with LME copper prices. SGH's position is a fixed-price receivable from KFH, not a copper holding. No write-down, no risk.
T·11MusharakahIn a Diminishing Musharakah on a KD 8M property (KFH 60%, SGH 40%), SGH buys KFH's share down to 35% after 2 years. How does the monthly rent SGH pays KFH change?
Explanation: Rent is charged only on KFH's remaining ownership share — because rent is the return for allowing someone else to use your asset. As KFH's share reduced from 60% to 35%, KFH owns less property — so KFH can only legitimately charge rent on what it owns. Month 1 rent based on 60%. Now based on 35% — approximately 58% of original rent. This declining rent mechanic is precisely what makes Diminishing Musharakah economically similar to a conventional amortising mortgage.

T·14ZakatZakat at SGH should be calculated as 2.5% of net profit for simplicity. Correct?

Your SSB must specifically certify the exact structure used — counterparties, commodity type, independence of transaction legs, pricing methodology.
