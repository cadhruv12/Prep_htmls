01 Global Pret — Ownership & Financials

Pret is a UK-founded sandwich/coffee chain, privately owned, currently going through a leveraged and somewhat strained chapter. This context shapes how the Kuwait franchisee thinks about cost discipline and royalty obligations — worth carrying into the conversation.

Why this matters The UK parent is leveraged and under review by its own owner. A franchisor in that position tends to push harder on royalty collection, brand-standard compliance, and reporting discipline from its franchisees — and may also be less flexible on terms if you ever need to renegotiate. Don't be surprised if the interview touches on "how do you manage the relationship/obligations with an overseas franchisor under financial pressure."

04 The Franchise Business Model — What Drives the P&L

Standard international F&B master-franchise economics. You won't have the exact Kuwait contract terms, but you should be able to speak fluently to the structure and the levers.

Typical Cost & Revenue Structure
LineDriver
RevenueStore-level sales — dine-in, takeaway, delivery aggregators (Talabat, Deliveroo — commission drag relevant here)
Royalty feeTypically a % of net sales payable to the franchisor (Pret UK / Pret Asia entity) — recurring, GBP or USD-denominated outflow against KWD revenue
Marketing/brand fundOften a separate % contribution, sometimes ring-fenced for regional or local marketing spend
Initial franchise feeOne-off per shop, capitalised and amortised over the franchise term
COGSCoffee (organic arabica — imported), bakery/sandwich inputs, packaging. Watch FX and customs duty on imported components
LabourLargest controllable opex in F&B retail — Kuwaiti labour law overtime, indemnity (end of service), and shift-pattern costs apply here
OccupancyMall vs. tower vs. standalone — percentage rent clauses common in GCC mall leases, worth flagging as a modelling consideration
CapexShop fit-out, kitchen equipment — capitalised, payback period tracked per unit

Unit economics framing to have ready

  • Revenue per shop vs. fit-out capex → implied payback period
  • Store-level EBITDA margin target range for quick-service/food-to-go in GCC malls (typically high teens to mid-20s% in mature locations)
  • Delivery-aggregator commission impact on margin (10–30% commission is standard, materially dilutes delivery-channel profitability)
  • How percentage-rent lease clauses behave at low vs. high sales volumes

06 Kuwait 5% Retention vs. KSA WHT/Zakat — Your Strongest Card

This is genuinely your differentiator in this interview, and it's stronger now that the role spans two markets — they need someone who can hold both compliance frameworks in their head simultaneously, not just one.

Two Different Regimes, Same Franchisor Relationship
AspectKuwaitSaudi Arabia
Regime5% tax retention, Law No. 2/2008Withholding Tax (WHT) under ZATCA — rate varies by payment type (royalties typically 15%, services 5–15% depending on classification), plus separate Zakat on Saudi/GCC-owned capital
Applies toPayments to any non-Kuwaiti contractor/service provider, including royalties to the UK/Asia franchisor
MechanicsRetain 5% of contract value until a No Liability Certificate (NOL) is obtained from KTA; retention released on clearanceWHT deducted at source on each payment to the non-resident franchisor and remitted to ZATCA monthly; no separate "retention pending clearance" concept — it's a direct withholding
RegistrationKTA registration for the contracting Kuwaiti entityZATCA registration; if One PM's KSA entity has any Saudi/GCC ownership, Zakat filing applies on top of WHT for the locally-owned portion
Treaty reliefGenerally limited — Kuwait's retention regime applies regardless of treaty in most practical cases unless specific clearance obtainedUK-Saudi double tax treaty may reduce royalty WHT rates if properly documented and certificate of residence obtained
Be ready to articulate, unprompted if needed
  • You understand both regimes apply to the same underlying relationship (royalty/fee payments to the Pret franchisor entity) but produce very different cash flow timing and compliance mechanics in each market
  • Kuwait: retention payable as a liability until NOL obtained; KSA: WHT is a point-in-time deduction at payment, no clearance-pending state
  • If One PM's KSA entity has mixed Saudi/foreign ownership, Zakat (on the Saudi-owned equity portion) and corporate income tax (on the foreign-owned portion) may both apply — worth flagging this nuance even if you don't have the exact ownership split
  • Whether the franchise agreement quotes royalty gross or net of local withholding in each jurisdiction — this affects who economically bears the tax cost

If they don't ask about this directly, look for an opening to raise it — contrasting the two regimes in the same breath signals genuine cross-market GCC tax fluency, not just textbook Kuwait knowledge.

07 Likely Interview Questions

Technical
How would you structure the accounting and tax treatment for royalty payments to an overseas franchisor — across both Kuwait and KSA?
Lead with the contrast: Kuwait's 5% retention/NOL mechanic vs. KSA's point-in-time WHT under ZATCA, plus possible Zakat exposure depending on KSA entity ownership. Tie back to franchise agreement terms (gross vs. net royalty quoting) in each market.
Operational
Walk me through how you'd build a store-level P&L for a multi-unit F&B operation across two countries.
Revenue by channel (dine-in/takeaway/delivery) → direct COGS → controllable opex (labour, utilities) → occupancy (incl. percentage rent if applicable) → store EBITDA → allocate franchise royalty/marketing fund → corporate overhead allocation → net contribution. Then layer market-level consolidation: Kuwait and KSA roll up separately before combining, given different currencies, tax regimes, and labour law.
Strategic
Pret's UK parent took a large impairment and is exploring a sale/IPO. How does that affect your thinking as the Kuwait/KSA master franchisee's finance lead?
Honest framing: parent-level leverage doesn't directly burden the local entities' balance sheets, but it can affect royalty rate negotiations, brand investment support, and supply chain terms if the parent tightens group-wide cost discipline. Worth monitoring franchise agreement renewal terms and any pass-through cost changes — more relevant now given a two-market master franchise has more at stake in that relationship than a single shop count would suggest.
FX/Treasury
How do you manage FX exposure across KWD and SAR functional currencies against GBP/USD obligations?
KWD is basket-pegged (USD-weighted but not 1:1), SAR is hard-pegged to USD — so the two markets don't carry identical FX risk even though both ultimately reference USD. Forward cover for known royalty payment dates if material; otherwise budget-rate monitoring and variance flagging by market.
Scope
Tell me about your multi-entity consolidation experience.
Direct strength — draw on your current/recent holding-group experience across multiple P&Ls and subsidiaries. Frame it generically (no employer naming) around consolidation mechanics, intercompany eliminations, and management reporting layered over a legal-entity structure — directly transferable to a Kuwait+KSA consolidation.

08 Questions to Ask Them

  • Does this role carry direct financial responsibility for both Kuwait and KSA, or is KSA locally led with this role consolidating at a regional level?
  • Team size and structure — centralised finance across both markets, or a finance lead in each country reporting into this role?
  • What's the current royalty and marketing fund structure with Pret's franchisor entity — fixed, or under renegotiation given the parent's financial position?
  • Is the finance function shared services across Al-Sayer Franchising's other brands (Caribou, Five Guys), or dedicated to One PM/Pret specifically?
  • What ERP/POS systems are in place in each market, and are Kuwait and KSA on a single instance or separate systems?
  • What's driving this hire — KSA growth, a gap from departure, or a new consolidated layer across the two markets?
  • Who does this role report to — Nabil Al-Ghoul, group finance at Al-Sayer Franchising, or elsewhere?

09 Read Between the Lines

Things to probe, not assume
  • A combined "Kuwait + KSA" mandate with a relatively small Kuwait base (7 shops) suggests KSA growth is the real reason for this hire — confirm current and planned KSA store count, since that's where the workload will actually sit
  • Don't assume KSA finance/compliance is already built out — if it's a newer market for One PM, you may be establishing ZATCA/WHT/Zakat processes from scratch rather than inheriting them
  • Franchise groups in Kuwait often run lean, multi-brand shared finance teams — confirm whether you'd be the sole finance lead across both markets or part of a larger Al-Sayer Franchising finance function
  • Given the parent's financial stress, ask plainly whether there's any pressure on franchisee royalty terms or brand investment support — a direct question, consistent with how you like to operate

10 Pre-Interview Checklist

  • Confirm exact role title, seniority, and reporting line before the interview if not already known
  • Confirm whether this role has direct P&L/consolidation responsibility for both Kuwait and KSA, or oversight of a locally-led KSA team
  • Prepare a 60–90 second articulation contrasting Kuwait's 5% retention/NOL mechanic with KSA's WHT/Zakat regime — your strongest differentiator now that both markets are in scope
  • Prepare a generic (no employer-named) summary of your multi-entity consolidation experience
  • Have 2–3 sharp questions ready from Section 8 — don't ask all of them, pick what fits the conversation
  • Know the headline numbers: £1.1bn group revenue, £166m adjusted profit, 717 shops globally, JAB ownership, 7 Kuwait shops, One PM as master franchisee for Kuwait/KSA/Qatar (Qatar sub-franchised to Al Mana)
  • Decide your honest answer to "why this move" — consistent with your current situation, no over-explaining
